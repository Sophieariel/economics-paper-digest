# China Economics Papers — 2026-04-22

## 1. The Chinese Current Account Imbalances: Puzzles, Patterns, and Possible Causes
**Authors:** Chang Ma, Shang-Jin Wei
**Date:** April 2026
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w35056
**Abstract:** China maintains a sizable current account surplus that has created tensions with trading partners. While sector-specific industrial and trade policies generate imbalances in particular industries, they contribute minimally to the overall economy-wide surplus. Instead, deeper structural elements—including demographic imbalances in gender ratios and disparities in access to credit between state-owned enterprises and private firms—represent the primary drivers of the macroeconomic imbalance. Short-term macroeconomic stimulus measures can increase imports and temporarily shrink the surplus, yet addressing the underlying issue permanently requires implementing structural reforms to tackle these fundamental problems.

## 2. A Tale of Two Countries – The Real Estate Crises in 1990s Japan and Contemporary China
**Authors:** Kenneth S. Rogoff, Yuanchen Yang
**Date:** April 2026
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w35054
**Abstract:** Real estate has been fundamental to China's economic expansion, but since 2018 its contribution has diminished substantially, shifting the sector from a growth driver into a significant constraint on economic activity. Policy tightening may have initiated the downturn, yet it stems from deeper structural problems in a sector that—including related upstream, downstream, and infrastructure activities—represents roughly one-third of total demand. With housing representing approximately 70 percent of household wealth, the ongoing price decline has produced substantial negative wealth impacts, intensifying the contraction through decreased consumption, investment, and sentiment. The authors document how China's real estate contraction propagates through the broader economy and assess prolonged stagnation risks if deterioration persists. They compare China's situation with Japan's 1990s real estate collapse, identifying noteworthy similarities in investment patterns and consumption reactions despite significant institutional variations. The research emphasizes real-side transmission channels, including amplification mechanisms beyond banking systems, in producing sustained economic losses following real estate downturns.

## 3. Consumer Tobacco Product Choices in China: The Impact of a Ban on Flavored E-cigarettes
**Authors:** Hua Wang, Yuhan Deng, Donald S. Kenkel, Alan D. Mathios, Sen Zeng
**Date:** April 2026
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w35048
**Abstract:** This study examines China's 2022 ban on flavored e-cigarettes through two discrete choice experiments conducted in 2021 and 2023, involving approximately 600 adult current smokers each. Researchers experimentally varied product prices and e-cigarette attributes to measure how flavor availability influences consumer preferences among cigarettes, e-cigarettes, and quitting. The findings indicate that banning flavored e-cigarettes decreases stated preferences for vaping but paradoxically increases stated preferences for traditional cigarettes. Notably, the predicted choice share of illegal flavored e-cigarettes under loose enforcement reaches 53% of the legal availability level, aligning with anecdotal evidence and survey data showing continued popularity of flavored products despite regulatory restrictions.

## 4. Household Migration and Collateral Constraint: Cash-based Housing Resettlement in China
**Authors:** Zhiguo He, Zehao Liu, Xinle Pang, Yang Su, Kunru Zou
**Date:** March 2026
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w34982
**Abstract:** The research examines how collateral constraints affect household migration patterns using China's shantytown renovation program (2015-2018) as a natural experiment. When cash replaced illiquid housing assets, households relocated to more expensive areas and contributed to price increases there. A dynamic spatial model with collateral constraints demonstrates that endogenous migration amplifies household housing expenditure responses by around 40% and accounts for over 20% of housing price growth during 2016-2020.

## 5. From Free Rider to Innovator: The Rise of China's Drug Development
**Authors:** Panle Jia Barwick, Hongyuan Xia, Tianli Xia
**Date:** March 2026
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w34977
**Abstract:** This study examines how China transformed from a pharmaceutical "free rider" into a worldwide innovator over recent years. In 2010, China represented under 8% of global clinical trials; by 2020, it exceeded the US in annual registered trial volume. Using a comprehensive database covering the drug development pipeline—including scientific publications, clinical trials, development milestones for China, the US, and Europe, plus sales data and government policies—researchers found that the National Reimbursement Drug List reform was the primary driver. This reform substantially grew the market for innovative medications. Following implementation, trial quantity increased 86%, with growth concentrated in reform-exposed disease areas and domestic firms developing first- or best-in-class drugs. The reform accounts for 43% of oncology trial activity growth, substantially outpacing knowledge accumulation and talent migration (24%). Long-term innovation gains from the reform exceeded static consumer access improvements by threefold.
