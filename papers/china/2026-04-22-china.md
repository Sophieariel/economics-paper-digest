# China Economics Papers — 2026-04-22

## 1. Consumer Tobacco Product Choices in China: The Impact of a Ban on Flavored E-cigarettes
**Authors:** Hua Wang, Yuhan Deng, Donald S. Kenkel, Alan D. Mathios, Sen Zeng
**Date:** April 2026
**Source:** NBER Working Paper 35048 — https://www.nber.org/papers/w35048
**Abstract:** The research examines China's 2022 ban on flavored e-cigarettes through discrete choice experiments conducted with approximately 600 adult smokers in 2021 and 2023. The study finds that a ban on flavored e-cigarettes decreases stated preferences for e-cigarettes but also has the unintended consequence of increasing stated preferences for cigarettes. Notably, the analysis predicts that flavored e-cigarettes maintain 53 percent of their legal market share even when prohibited but loosely enforced, aligning with observed market evidence that these products remained popular following the regulatory action.

## 2. The Chinese Current Account Imbalances: Puzzles, Patterns, and Possible Causes
**Authors:** Chang Ma, Shang-Jin Wei
**Date:** April 2026
**Source:** NBER Working Paper 35056 — https://www.nber.org/papers/w35056
**Abstract:** The paper examines China's substantial current account surplus and its sources. While trade and industrial policies create imbalances at the sector level, they have a relatively limited role in the economy-wide surplus. Instead, structural factors—including an unbalanced sex ratio and unequal financing access between state-owned and non-state enterprises—are identified as more significant drivers. The authors conclude that macroeconomic stimulus may temporarily reduce the surplus through increased imports, but addressing the underlying structural issues is necessary for a lasting solution.

## 3. When Do Financial Frictions Matter for Misallocation?
**Authors:** Yan Bai, Dan Lu, Xu Tian, Yajie Wang
**Date:** March 2026
**Source:** NBER Working Paper 34930 — https://www.nber.org/papers/w34930
**Abstract:** The research reassesses how financial frictions contribute to capital misallocation using a model informed by firm-level borrowing costs and average revenue product of capital (ARPK). Using Chinese manufacturing data, the authors document substantial dispersion in ARPK alongside a strong positive relationship between ARPK and the borrowing costs firms face. Removing financial frictions alone raises TFP by 25 percentage points when other capital distortions exist, but less than 2 percentage points without them. The study reveals that financial frictions can be highly distortionary when other sources of misallocation are present. NBER program codes: Economic Fluctuations and Growth; working group: Chinese Economy.

## 4. AI Patents in the United States and China: Measurement, Organization, and Knowledge Flows
**Authors:** Hanming Fang, Xian Gu, Hanyin Yan, Wu Zhu
**Date:** April 2026
**Source:** NBER Working Paper 35022 — https://www.nber.org/papers/w35022
**Abstract:** The researchers develop a machine-learning classifier to identify artificial intelligence patents, achieving 97.0 percent precision, 91.3 percent recall, and a 94.0 percent F1 score. Analyzing U.S. patents (1976–2023) and Chinese patents (2010–2023), they find rapid growth in AI patenting with convergence between countries, though China recently surpassed the U.S. in annual counts. Organizational structures differ significantly: U.S. innovation concentrates among large private firms in established hubs, while Chinese innovation is more geographically diffuse and institutionally diverse, with larger roles for universities and state-owned enterprises. AI patents provide market-value premiums in both countries, and citation patterns reveal continued technological interdependence rather than decoupling. NBER program codes: Productivity, Innovation, and Entrepreneurship; working group: Chinese Economy.

## 5. Homemade Foreign Trading
**Authors:** Zhiguo He, Yuehan Wang, Xiaoquan Zhu
**Date:** April 2026
**Source:** NBER Working Paper 35095 — https://www.nber.org/papers/w35095
**Abstract:** The research examines cross-border holding data from all custodians in China's Stock Connect to demonstrate that mainland insiders circumvent oversight through round-tripping. Following a 2018 reform on investor identification, the predictive power of northbound flows diminished, alongside reduced correlation with insider trading. The effect was strongest among less established foreign custodians. The study concludes that the reform erodes price informativeness, particularly in stocks with high exposure to homemade foreign investors, underscoring regulatory cooperation's importance in market integration. NBER program codes: Asset Pricing, Corporate Finance, International Finance and Macroeconomics, International Trade and Investment.
