# China Economics Papers — 2026-04-27

## 1. China's Global Ownership
**Authors:** Jennie Bai, Luc Laeven, Yaojun Ke, Hong Ru
**Date:** April 2026
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w35106 (NBER Working Paper No. 35106)
**Abstract:** We study the global footprint and real effects of Chinese overseas corporate ownership. By assembling a comprehensive micro-level dataset of 161,773 firms across 159 countries (2012–2021), we independently reconstruct multi-layered ownership chains to trace capital through offshore tax havens to its ultimate origin. This approach reveals a global footprint substantially broader than official FDI statistics. Chinese-controlled foreign assets expanded at 20% annually, reaching $2.1 trillion or roughly 3% of global corporate assets by 2021. Chinese investors—particularly state-owned enterprises (SOEs)—strategically target R&D-intensive and supply-chain-linked firms. Following acquisition, target firms increase capital stock and R&D expenditures, yet these inputs fail to generate higher patent output and are accompanied by a significant decline in profitability. We document a novel 'innovation spillback' mechanism: while target innovation remains stagnant, Chinese parent firms experience a sharp acceleration in granted patents following their first developed-economy acquisition. Furthermore, a greater Chinese presence crowds out R&D at non-target peer firms, though aggregate industry-level innovation remains unchanged. China thus represents a distinctively state-driven model of global ownership that accepts weaker near-term performance to internalize technological capacity at home.

## 2. Raising grievances to the state: The political economic effects of anti-corruption crackdowns on labor activism in China
**Authors:** Huiyi Chen
**Date:** 2026 (Journal of Development Economics, Volume 181, article 103758)
**Source:** https://ideas.repec.org/a/eee/deveco/v181y2026ics0304387826000416.html
**Abstract:** This paper examines how government actions influence workers' incentives to strike, using China's anti-corruption initiative as a natural experiment. The paper develops a model of a worker's decision to strike in which higher firm–government corruption decreases the worker's expected return to striking. Combining a unique dataset on city-level labor strikes with the rollout of high-profile corruption inspections from 2011–2020, the analysis shows that strike incidents rise sharply after a city's first high-profile inspection: strikes roughly double within one year and triple within two years. The increases are concentrated in private construction and manufacturing firms, are driven primarily by wage arrears, and propagate through worker network effects. Inspections appear to weaken firm–government connections, redirect official attention toward worker grievances, and raise workers' anticipated payoffs from collective action, illustrating how anti-corruption enforcement reshapes the political-economic equilibrium between firms, workers, and the state.

## 3. English's significance: Exam performance by subject and future income in China
**Authors:** Ruixue Jia, Hongbin Li, Yu Qin, Qiuyi Wang, Jing Wu
**Date:** 2026 (Journal of Development Economics, Volume 181)
**Source:** https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0304387826000398 (DOI: 10.1016/j.jdeveco.2026.103756)
**Abstract:** Using administrative data on roughly 190,000 individuals born between 1980 and 1985, this paper studies how subject-specific scores on China's National College Entrance Examination (gaokao) predict labor-market earnings over the life cycle. Although Chinese, Math, and English test scores are all positively correlated with future income, English emerges as the most influential predictor and remains so across different career stages. The English–income relationship operates primarily through employer demand from foreign-invested enterprises, indicating that returns to English skills reflect the value of these skills in the segment of China's economy most exposed to globalization. The findings highlight the enduring labor-market value of English proficiency in the largest developing country to integrate rapidly into the world economy and have implications for education policy in other globalizing developing economies.

## 4. Trade wars and rumors of trade wars: The dynamic effects of the U.S.–China tariff hikes
**Authors:** Trang T. Hoang, Carter Mix
**Date:** 2026 (Journal of International Economics, forthcoming; DOI 10.1016/j.jinteco.2026.104229)
**Source:** https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=5101245
**Abstract:** We use a general equilibrium dynamic trade model to study the effects of the U.S.–China tariff hikes on the global economy. Our model captures key trade patterns since the tariff hikes, such as the pace of the decline in U.S.–China bilateral trade and the reallocation of global imports from the U.S. toward China. The welfare losses from the tariff hikes are much larger in our dynamic model than implied by static trade models. Expectations about the persistence of the tariff hikes shape aggregate dynamics in the short to medium run, so that announcements and rumors of future tariff changes generate substantial effects on trade flows, investment, and welfare even before the tariffs themselves take effect.

## 5. Higher education expansion and the rural–urban gap in secondary schooling and labor outcomes: Evidence from China's 1999 reform
**Authors:** Ruoming Zhang
**Date:** 2026 (Journal of Development Economics, Volume 181)
**Source:** https://ideas.repec.org/s/eee/deveco.html (Journal of Development Economics, Volume 181, 2026)
**Abstract:** This paper examines how China's 1999 higher education expansion reshaped secondary schooling and labor-market outcomes for rural and urban youth. Exploiting cross-cohort and cross-province variation in exposure to the rapid expansion in college admissions, the analysis shows that the reform substantially raised secondary school enrollment and completion rates, with disproportionately large effects in rural areas where prior attainment was low. The induced increase in human capital is reflected in higher subsequent wages, more skilled occupational sorting, and increased non-agricultural employment among rural cohorts exposed to the expansion. While the policy narrowed long-run rural–urban gaps in secondary schooling and labor-market outcomes, gains in access to elite higher education and to the highest-earning positions remained concentrated among urban students, suggesting that mass higher-education expansion can reduce broad-based educational inequality even as it leaves residual stratification at the top of the distribution.
