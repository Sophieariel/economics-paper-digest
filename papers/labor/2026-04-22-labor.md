# Labor Economics Papers — 2026-04-22

## 1. Immigrants at the Margin: Labor Market Effects of the Minimum Wage
**Authors:** Mark Borgschulte, Heepyung Cho, Darren Lubotsky
**Date:** April 2026
**Source:** NBER Working Paper 35038 — https://www.nber.org/papers/w35038
**Abstract:** The paper examines how minimum wage increases affect immigrant versus native workers differently. The authors find that minimum wage increases lead to reduced hours of work among immigrants with no effect on their employment. These impacts concentrate among recently-arrived, likely-undocumented workers in high-turnover industries, while native workers remain unaffected even in similar circumstances. The authors conclude that affected immigrant labor markets involve low-surplus, low-investment employment relationships in which hours adjust flexibly yet face unusually high replacement costs. NBER program codes: Labor Studies, Public Economics, Urban Economics.

## 2. Why Do Americans No Longer Work So Much More Than Non-Americans?
**Authors:** Serdar Birinci, Loukas Karabarbounis, Kurt See
**Date:** April 2026
**Source:** NBER Working Paper 35020 — https://www.nber.org/papers/w35020
**Abstract:** The authors document that the significant gap in working hours between Americans and non-Americans that existed in the 1990s has partially reversed. They develop an economic model incorporating individual heterogeneity, household dynamics, and tax/benefit systems to examine this convergence. Their findings indicate that U.S. hours per person declined after 2000 owing mainly to the rise of government health benefits provided to the non-employed. By contrast, other countries experienced rising labor supply driven by wage increases and changing preferences toward work, while their non-employment benefits remained comparatively stable. NBER program codes: Economic Fluctuations and Growth, Labor Studies, Public Economics.

## 3. A Gender Quota for Top Executives: Diversity without Disruption
**Authors:** David A. Matsa, Amalia R. Miller
**Date:** April 2026
**Source:** NBER Working Paper 35030 — https://www.nber.org/papers/w35030
**Abstract:** The researchers examine Germany's requirement that major public companies include at least one woman on their top executive teams. The quota increased female representation among top executives by approximately two-thirds. Companies recruited women from outside their established networks, often selecting candidates without prior public-company experience over similarly-qualified male candidates. Most appointments went to HR or specialized roles, with no increase in female CEOs. The study found no significant effects on female managers in lower positions, gender equality policies, firm value, or performance metrics, concluding the quota successfully boosted diversity without causing much disruption. NBER program codes: Corporate Finance, Law and Economics, Labor Studies; working groups: Gender in the Economy, Personnel Economics.

## 4. The Effects of California's $20 Fast Food Minimum Wage on Prices
**Authors:** Jeffrey Clemens, Olivia Edwards, Jonathan Meer, Joshua D. Nguyen
**Date:** March 2026
**Source:** NBER Working Paper 34990 — https://www.nber.org/papers/w34990
**Abstract:** The researchers examine California's $20 fast food minimum wage legislation (Assembly Bill 1228), which took effect in April 2024. Using Consumer Price Index data across 21 metropolitan areas, they find that food-away-from-home prices in California's four sampled regions rose between 3.3 and 3.6 percent relative to 17 comparison areas through December 2024. Placebo tests on unaffected product categories show no differential increases. The investigators attribute the price rises to spillovers to the full-service sector, as well as changes in the production functions and product quality choices of limited-service restaurants. NBER program codes: Labor Studies, Public Economics; working group: Personnel Economics.

## 5. Birthright Citizenship and Youth Crime
**Authors:** Leander Andres, Stefan Bauernschuster, Gordon B. Dahl, Helmut Rainer, Simone Schüller
**Date:** April 2026
**Source:** NBER Working Paper 35070 — https://www.nber.org/papers/w35070
**Abstract:** The study examines how birthright citizenship affects youth criminal behavior, utilizing a German policy reform that granted automatic citizenship to eligible immigrant children born after January 1, 2000. Using administrative crime data from three German federal states, the researchers find that immigrant youth who acquired citizenship at birth are substantially less likely to engage in criminal activity, with estimates indicating a 70 percent reduction in crime. The authors suggest that inclusive citizenship policies may lower crime rates and associated costs while potentially strengthening social cohesion. NBER program codes: Children and Families, Labor Studies, Public Economics; working group: Economics of Crime.
