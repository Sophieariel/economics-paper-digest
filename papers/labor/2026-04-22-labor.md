# Labor Economics Papers — 2026-04-22

## 1. Immigrants at the Margin: Labor Market Effects of the Minimum Wage [Gender/Labor]
**Authors:** Mark Borgschulte, Heepyung Cho, Darren Lubotsky
**Date:** April 2026
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w35038
**Abstract:** The paper examines differential effects of minimum wages on immigrant and native workers in the United States. Key findings indicate minimum wage increases reduce immigrant work hours without affecting employment, particularly among recently-arrived workers in high-turnover sectors. Native workers demonstrate no comparable response. The authors characterize affected immigrant labor markets as low-surplus, low-investment employment relationships with flexible hours embedded in markets where worker replacement carries unusually high costs.

## 2. High Schools and the Uneven Rise in American Opportunity [Education]
**Authors:** Alison Doxey, Ezra Karger, Peter Nencka
**Date:** April 2026
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w35068
**Abstract:** During the period from 1850 to 1910, access to high schools in America expanded dramatically, with the proportion of young people living in towns offering secondary education growing from 17% to 46%. This research examines how that expansion affected economic prospects using comprehensive historical data on American high schools. The study demonstrates that opening a high school substantially increased school attendance among adolescents in those communities compared to nearby areas without such institutions. For women specifically, expanded secondary education increased women's labor force participation and job quality, while reducing the probability of early marriage and early childbearing. The research indicates that high school availability accounted for approximately one-third of the growth in women's workforce engagement between 1870 and 1930. However, the benefits were not equally distributed. The greatest positive impacts accrued to children from wealthy families, while Black students generally did not benefit. Though secondary education helped narrow gender-based economic disparities, it simultaneously expanded existing inequalities based on race and social class.

## 3. The Empathy Channel in Fertility [Fertility]
**Authors:** Sebastian Galiani, Raul A. Sosa
**Date:** April 2026
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w35021
**Abstract:** The authors propose that exposure to infants in the social environment activates neurobiological mechanisms that increase the desire for parenthood. As infant populations decline, this stimulus weakens, further reducing fertility motivation. They integrate this mechanism into an economic model showing it creates positive externalities—each birth increases others' desire for children, making unregulated markets inefficient. Their analysis reveals the standard Pigouvian subsidy approach significantly exceeds the economically optimal rate. Using calibrated estimates, they find the empathy channel may explain roughly 3-33% of observed fertility declines (13.4% baseline), with optimal subsidies generating modest welfare gains of approximately 0.22% in consumption-equivalent terms.

## 4. Can Personal Access to Medical Expertise Overcome Vaccine Hesitancy? [Health]
**Authors:** D. Mark Anderson, Ron Diris, Raymond Montizaan, Daniel I. Rees
**Date:** April 2026
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w35019
**Abstract:** The researchers examined Dutch medical school applicants and their older family members (ages 60+) to investigate how access to physicians influences vaccination decisions. Using lottery-based admission variation as a natural experiment, they found that having a physician in the family increases the likelihood of complying with government recommendations for COVID-19 booster doses. The analysis revealed notably stronger effects when the family physician was female, pointing to gender-related differences in medical communication effectiveness.

## 5. Exploring Trends in Severe Maternal Morbidity in the U.S. [Health]
**Authors:** Pinka Chatterji, Fangning Li, Sara Markowitz
**Date:** April 2026
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w35062
**Abstract:** The research examines rising severe maternal morbidity (SMM) in America, particularly among Black mothers. Using inpatient data from 1998-2019, the authors analyze how maternal, hospital, and county-level characteristics may be associated with both the rising trends and the racial disparity in SMM. Their decomposition analysis reveals that observed characteristics account for minimal overall SMM variation but substantially explain Black-White disparities. The findings highlight medical and pregnancy comorbidities as primary drivers and suggest focusing on patients with comorbid conditions and delving more deeply into the features of hospital environments.
