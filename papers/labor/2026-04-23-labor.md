# Labor Economics Papers — 2026-04-23

## 1. A Gender Quota for Top Executives: Diversity without Disruption [Gender]
**Authors:** David A. Matsa and Amalia R. Miller
**Date:** April 2026
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w35030
**Abstract:** The research examines Germany's mandate requiring major public companies to include at least one woman among top executives. The policy expanded female representation in these positions by roughly two-thirds. Companies primarily recruited women from external sources without prior experience in top-executive roles at public firms, selecting them over similarly qualified male candidates. Most appointees received HR or specialized positions, with no rise in female CEOs. The study found no meaningful changes in female manager representation at lower organizational levels, gender equality initiatives, company valuation, or operational results. "The quota boosted diversity without causing much disruption." Program tags: Corporate Finance; Law and Economics; Labor Studies; Gender in the Economy; Personnel Economics.

## 2. The Impact of Maternal Education on Early Childhood Development [Education]
**Authors:** Moriam Khanam, Mohammad Hajizadeh, and Casey Warman
**Date:** April 2026
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w35075
**Abstract:** The researchers examined how maternal schooling affects young children's development using a female secondary school stipend program in rural Bangladesh as a natural experiment. Drawing on 2019 survey data, they found that five years of stipend eligibility raised mothers' education by approximately one year. Their analysis revealed that "an additional year of maternal education improves early childhood development scores by 0.5 points on a scale of 0-10," with notable gains in developmental readiness and literacy-numeracy domains. The study also identified improvements in child nutrition, home learning environments, and parenting practices as key mechanisms through which maternal education benefits children. Program tags: Children and Families; Development Economics; Economics of Education; Economics of Health.

## 3. Bad News and Policy Views: Expectations, Disappointment, and Opposition to Affirmative Action [Education]
**Authors:** Louis-Pierre Lepage, Heather Sarsons, and Michael Thaler
**Date:** April 2026
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w35045
**Abstract:** The research investigates how college applicants' admissions outcomes relative to their expectations influence views on affirmative action. Among recent White and Asian applicants, those who received fewer acceptances than anticipated were "relatively more likely to believe that affirmative action played an important role in their admissions outcomes" and showed the strongest opposition to such policies. A complementary experiment with parents of prospective applicants found that providing disappointing admissions forecasts caused overconfident parents to increase opposition to affirmative action and donate to anti-affirmative action organizations. The findings suggest individuals attribute unfavorable outcomes to external factors, particularly policies benefiting out-groups. Program tags: Labor Studies; Race and Stratification in the Economy.

## 4. The Emergence of the Poverty–Mental Health Gradient and the Great Pakistan Earthquake of 2005 [Health]
**Authors:** Tahir Andrabi, Benjamin Daniels, and Jishnu Das
**Date:** April 2026
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w35060
**Abstract:** The research examines why the relationship between poverty and mental health differs significantly across populations. Using data from 126 villages near Pakistan's 2005 earthquake fault line, researchers found that in unaffected areas, no correlation existed between consumption poverty and mental health. However, "in unaffected populations there is no correlation between consumption poverty and mental" health, while "among those nearest the fault, mental health improves by 0.12 standard deviations per log unit of consumption." The gradient disappeared beyond 20 kilometers from the epicenter. These findings suggest that mass adversity may activate the poverty-mental health relationship. Program tags: Development Economics; Economics of Health; Health, Education, and Welfare.

## 5. The Impacts of Medicaid's Managed Long-Term Services and Supports on Health Outcomes in Medicare [Health]
**Authors:** Ajin Lee, Maya Rossin-Slater, Becky Staiger, and Amanda Su
**Date:** April 2026
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w35055
**Abstract:** The research examines how transitioning to managed long-term services and supports (MLTSS) affects dual-eligible beneficiaries aged 65+ in Florida and New York. Using Medicare claims data and a differences-in-differences approach based on "county-by-county MLTSS rollouts," the study finds divergent results: "MLTSS leads to a 4.2 percent increase in hospitalizations in Florida, but no significant change in New York." The authors suggest declining flu vaccination rates in Florida may explain increased respiratory hospitalizations, highlighting state-level variation in program effects. Program tags: Economics of Aging; Economics of Health.
