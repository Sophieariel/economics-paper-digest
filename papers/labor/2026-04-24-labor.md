# Labor Economics Papers — 2026-04-24

## 1. High Schools and the Uneven Rise in American Opportunity [Gender]
**Authors:** Alison Doxey, Ezra Karger, and Peter Nencka
**Date:** April 2026
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w35068
**Abstract:** Between 1850 and 1910, high school access expanded dramatically across the United States. This research demonstrates that while this expansion "transformed economic opportunities for many young adults," it simultaneously "widened class and racial inequalities." The study finds that high schools increased women's labor force participation and improved job quality while reducing early marriage rates. However, the benefits accrued primarily to children from wealthy families, and Black children did not experience average gains. The research shows high schools substantially narrowed gender gaps but widened existing race and class disparities. Program tags: Children and Families; Development of the American Economy; Economics of Education; Labor Studies; Public Economics; Gender in the Economy (Working Group).

## 2. Skill Formation, Child Labor, and the Schooling Consequences of the World War I Agricultural Boom [Education]
**Authors:** Taylor Jaworski, Carl T. Kitchens, and Luke P. Rodgers
**Date:** April 2026
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w35032
**Abstract:** The research examines how agricultural commodity price increases during World War I influenced human capital development in early 20th-century America. Using newly compiled county-level enrollment statistics, the authors document significant declines in school participation during the boom's peak. Analysis of linked census records from 1910-1940 reveals that teenage exposure reduced final educational attainment by approximately 0.27 to 0.47 years, with effects concentrated at the secondary level. Notably, outcomes for younger children varied by region: higher family income boosted schooling in some areas but was negated where child labor remained common. The findings suggest dynamic skill complementarities whose long-term educational impacts depend on local labor market conditions. Program tags: Development of the American Economy; Labor Studies.

## 3. The Labor Market Returns to Delaying Pregnancy [Fertility]
**Authors:** Yana Gallen, Juanna Schrøter Joensen, Eva Rye Johansen, and Gregory Veramendi
**Date:** April 2026
**Source:** https://www.iza.org/publications/dp/18529/the-labor-market-returns-to-delaying-pregnancy
**Abstract:** The research examines labor market consequences of unplanned pregnancies among women using reversible contraceptives. Using Swedish linked health and employment data, the authors find that unplanned pregnancies significantly disrupt career advancement, causing income reductions of 19% within five years. The effects prove particularly severe for younger women and those pursuing education. The study leverages pregnancy timing as a quasi-random variation to establish causal effects of unplanned births on subsequent earnings trajectories. IZA Discussion Paper No. 18529.

## 4. The Well-Being Effects of Digital Mental Health Care [Health]
**Authors:** Manuela Angelucci, Raissa Fabregas, and Antonia Vazquez
**Date:** April 2026
**Source:** https://www.iza.org/publications/dp/18538/the-well-being-effects-of-digital-mental-health-care
**Abstract:** The researchers conducted a randomized controlled trial with 1,964 Mexican women experiencing psychological distress to evaluate an AI-powered mental health application. Over six months, the app "improved mental health by 0.3 standard deviations with no evidence of harm," while also enhancing sleep quality, promoting healthier behaviors, and reducing work absences. The benefits substantially exceeded costs. Notably, treated participants showed increased interest in seeking traditional psychotherapy, yet this factor did not account for the majority of improvements. Although app engagement declined after the initial month—a typical pattern for digital interventions—the positive effects remained. The authors conclude that "even short-term engagement can produce durable improvements through sustained behavioral change" as participants continued practicing techniques the app promoted. IZA Discussion Paper No. 18538.

## 5. Mind the Confidence Gap: Gender, Domain-Specific Self-Beliefs, and STEM Pathways [Gender]
**Authors:** Britta Hecker, Nikki Shure, and Ipek Yükselen
**Date:** April 2026
**Source:** https://www.iza.org/publications/dp/18535/mind-the-confidence-gap-gender-domain-specific-self-beliefs-and-stem-pathways
**Abstract:** The researchers examined how adolescents' subject-specific confidence influences their participation in STEM fields using longitudinal German cohort data. They found that "domain-specific confidence measures provide markedly different predictions from composite confidence indices." Specifically, greater math and ICT confidence boosted STEM pathway entry, while stronger reading confidence reduced it. The study emphasizes that analyzing confidence by individual domain rather than as an aggregated measure reveals nuanced patterns that broad interventions might otherwise overlook and potentially worsen existing gender gaps in STEM participation. IZA Discussion Paper No. 18535. Keywords: education, STEM, confidence, gender.
