# Labor Economics Papers — 2026-04-25

## 1. Special Education Substantially Improves Learning: Evidence from Three States [Education]
**Authors:** Stephanie Coffey, Joshua Goodman, Amy Ellen Schwartz, Leanna Stiefel, Marcus A. Winters, and Yunee H. Yoon
**Date:** April 2026
**Source:** https://www.iza.org/publications/dp/18531/special-education-substantially-improves-learning-evidence-from-three-states
**Abstract:** The research examines how individualized educational supports affect student achievement using longitudinal administrative data from Massachusetts, Indiana, and Connecticut. Using an event-study design that tracks student achievement around the timing of special education classification, the authors document a V-shaped pattern in which "scores decline prior to placement and rise sharply afterward," with achievement gains of 0.2–0.4 standard deviations within three years. The study demonstrates that individualized supports substantially enhance learning productivity across different disability categories and student subgroups, with gains persisting under conservative assumptions and independent of testing accommodations. The findings push back against narratives that special education programs fail to improve learning, instead suggesting that individualized services can produce sizable, durable academic gains. IZA Discussion Paper No. 18531. JEL: I21, I28, H52, J24. Keywords: special education, human capital, treatment effects, education policy.

## 2. Do Reforms Aimed at Reducing Time to Graduation Work? Evidence from the Italian Higher Education System [Education]
**Authors:** Davide Malacrino, Samuel Nocito, and Raffaele Saggio
**Date:** April 2026
**Source:** https://www.iza.org/publications/dp/18530/do-reforms-aimed-at-reducing-time-to-graduation-work-evidence-from-the-italian-higher-education-system
**Abstract:** The paper evaluates "a reform aimed at expediting graduation times in Italian universities by reducing the number of exams" required for completion. Leveraging the staggered implementation of the reform across institutions in an event-study/quasi-experimental design, the authors find that the policy increased on-time graduation but initially decreased post-graduation employment probability. However, "this negative effect reverses into a positive one in the medium run," explained by students using the freed-up time to pursue "additional educational qualifications" such as master's degrees. The results suggest that policies designed to shorten time-to-degree have important dynamic effects on subsequent human capital investment and labor market outcomes that are not captured in short-run evaluations. IZA Discussion Paper No. 18530. JEL: I23, I26, I28, J22. Keywords: higher education policy evaluation, time to graduation, labor outcomes.

## 3. Mitigating the Consequences of Job Loss in Lower-Income Countries: Evidence from Ethiopia [Gender]
**Authors:** Lukas Hensel, Girum Abebe, Francois Gerard, and Stefano Caria
**Date:** April 2026
**Source:** https://www.iza.org/publications/dp/18537/mitigating-the-consequences-of-job-loss-in-lower-income-countries-evidence-from-ethiopia
**Abstract:** The study examines how job displacement affects formal-sector workers in lower-income economies, focusing on female factory workers displaced by a trade-related shock in Ethiopia's industrial parks. Combining a quasi-experimental analysis of a tariff shock with a randomized experiment that varies the structure of cash assistance, the authors document "lasting declines in employment and consumption spending, and rising poverty" among displaced women. They show that lump-sum job-loss payments encourage immediate spending but reduce long-term employment, while distributing equivalent amounts "in tranches improves consumption smoothing and avoids adverse employment effects." The findings highlight how the design of job-loss insurance shapes labor supply decisions among women in low-income labor markets and inform the design of social protection programs in settings where formal unemployment insurance is absent. IZA Discussion Paper No. 18537. JEL: O12, J63, J65, I32, O14, J16. Keywords: job loss, job-loss insurance, trade shock.

## 4. The Effect of Annuities on Longevity [Health]
**Authors:** Borja Larrain, Alessandro Previtero, and Felipe Severino
**Date:** April 2026
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w35082
**Abstract:** This research investigates how annuitization influences mortality outcomes using administrative data on approximately 600,000 Chilean retirees between 2004 and 2022. The authors employ recent stock market returns at the time of retirement as an instrument for the annuitization decision, motivated by retirees' extrapolative beliefs about future returns, which generate quasi-random variation in whether retirees choose an annuity rather than phased withdrawals. Their estimates show that "the decision to annuitize substantially reduces mortality at five- and ten-year horizons." The protective mechanism appears to operate by shielding retirees from income volatility and investment-related stress: survey data indicate annuitants increase health investments and report lower disability rates. The findings document a novel link between retirement-income design, financial stress, and longevity, with direct implications for pension and social security policy. NBER Working Paper No. 35082. Program tags: Economics of Aging; Economics of Health; Behavioral Finance; Household Finance; Insurance.

## 5. Levelling up? The Role of Need and Merit Based University Grants in Non-Selective Higher Education [Education]
**Authors:** Daniela Sonedda, Marcello Matranga, Gianluigi Vernasca, Mariacristina Rossi, and Francesco Figari
**Date:** April 2026
**Source:** https://www.iza.org/publications/dp/18547/levelling-up-the-role-of-need-and-merit-based-university-grants-in-non-selective-higher-education
**Abstract:** The authors examine "the interaction between need- and merit-based university grants in a non-selective higher education system." Using rich administrative data from a northern Italian university, they apply a difference-in-differences estimator designed for multiple treatments, separately analyzing students entering and exiting need- and merit-based eligibility categories. The analysis documents a tension between the two grant types, where merit thresholds operate as screening mechanisms that interact with need-based support. The grants tend to benefit disadvantaged yet academically capable students and improve enrollment and academic performance, but generate "perverse incentive effects that vary by gender" and substantial attrition among initial recipients. The results have implications for the joint design of need- and merit-based aid in non-selective higher-education systems and highlight gendered responses to financial-aid incentives. IZA Discussion Paper No. 18547. JEL: I22, I23, J24. Keywords: university grants, educational outcomes, non-selective higher education.
