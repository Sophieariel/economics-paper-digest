# Labor Economics Papers — 2026-04-26

## 1. Gender Mix and Team Performance: Evidence from Obstetrics [Gender]
**Authors:** Ambar La Forgia and Manasvini Singh
**Date:** April 2026
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w35084
**Abstract:** This paper examines how the gender composition of professional teams affects performance in a high-stakes clinical setting, using administrative data on roughly 2.5 million U.S. births. The authors leverage the quasi-random assignment of patients to two-member obstetrician teams to identify the causal effect of team gender mix on maternal outcomes. They document that "female-only teams achieve optimal maternal outcomes, while all-male teams show the poorest results," with severe maternal complications occurring 15.8% more often in male-only teams than in female-only teams. Mixed-gender teams led by women perform 7.1–10.8% worse than female-only teams, suggesting that gender norms shape team decision-making and that traditional hierarchies can invert under stress. Strikingly, female-only teams are "the only team type to have no racial disparity in maternal outcomes" for Black mothers. The findings link occupational gender composition and within-team dynamics directly to productivity and disparities in a setting with life-or-death consequences. NBER Working Paper No. 35084. Program: Economics of Health.

## 2. Work from Home and Fertility [Fertility]
**Authors:** Steven J. Davis, Cevat Giray Aksoy, Jose Maria Barrero, Nicholas Bloom, Katelyn Cranney, Mathias Dolls, and Pablo Zarate
**Date:** March 2026
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w34963
**Abstract:** The authors examine "how fertility relates to work from home (WFH) in the post-pandemic era" using cross-country survey data on workers and their partners. They document that realized and planned fertility are higher among individuals who work from home at least one day per week, and that the boost is largest when both partners do so. Estimates imply that lifetime fertility is about 0.32 children per woman higher when both partners work from home compared with neither, with effects concentrated among college-educated workers and dual-earner couples. The paper interprets the pattern through a household time-use mechanism: WFH reduces commuting and rigid schedules, lowering the time cost of childrearing for parents on a career track. The findings imply that durable shifts in remote work — a feature of the post-pandemic labor market — could meaningfully affect aggregate fertility and the supply of labor in advanced economies. NBER Working Paper No. 34963. Programs: Labor Studies; Children and Families; Economic Fluctuations and Growth; Public Economics; Productivity, Innovation, and Entrepreneurship.

## 3. The Math-Verbal Divide: Unequal Returns to Cognitive Skills in Education and Work [Education]
**Authors:** Judith M. Delaney and Paul J. Devereux
**Date:** April 2026
**Source:** https://www.iza.org/publications/dp/18542/the-math-verbal-divide-unequal-returns-to-cognitive-skills-in-education-and-work
**Abstract:** Using administrative data on English secondary-school students linked to higher-education and earnings records through age 34, the authors decompose the returns to cognitive skills into separate math and verbal components. They find sharply different roles for the two skill domains: "verbal skills strongly predict educational attainment...while mathematical skills yield substantially larger earnings returns." At ages 30–34, individuals at the 75th percentile of math scores earn roughly 29% more than those at the 25th percentile, compared with about 14% for verbal skills. Decompositions show that field of study explains a large share of the gap — verbally skilled students sort into fields with higher completion but lower wages, while mathematically skilled students sort into STEM and other high-paying disciplines. The results imply that pooled measures of cognitive ability mask important heterogeneity in the labor market, and that policies designed to raise college completion may not translate into earnings gains unless they shift the field-of-study margin. IZA Discussion Paper No. 18542. JEL: I26, I24, I21. Keywords: math skills, verbal skills, college field of study, STEM.

## 4. Hospital Billing Regulations and Financial Well-Being: Evidence from California's Fair Pricing Law [Health]
**Authors:** Yaa Akosa Antwi, Marion Aouad, and Nathan E. Blascak
**Date:** April 2026
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w35080
**Abstract:** This paper estimates the financial-health effects of regulating what hospitals can charge low- and moderate-income patients. The authors study California's 2007 Fair Pricing Law, which capped the prices that nonprofit hospitals can bill uninsured and underinsured patients meeting income thresholds, and use county-level variation in the size of the uninsured population as a source of differential exposure to the policy. They find that the regulation "reduces the likelihood of incurring non-medical debt in collections and the number of non-medical accounts in collections," with corresponding improvements in credit scores and reductions in delinquencies for residents of more exposed counties. The paper provides direct evidence that hospital pricing regulation — a key but understudied lever in U.S. health policy — can produce measurable gains in household financial stability and, by reducing medical debt spillovers into other obligations, may relieve constraints that affect labor supply and job search behavior. NBER Working Paper No. 35080. Programs: Economics of Health; Household Finance; Financial Economics; Health, Education, and Welfare.

## 5. Restricting Temporary Contracts Increases Firm-Provided Training: Evidence from Spain [Education]
**Authors:** Pawel Adrjan, Jonas Jessen, and Carlos Victoria Lanzón
**Date:** April 2026
**Source:** https://www.iza.org/publications/dp/18539/restricting-temporary-contracts-increases-firm-provided-training-evidence-from-spain
**Abstract:** The paper studies how regulatory restrictions on temporary employment shape firms' incentives to invest in worker human capital. Using "3.1 million online job postings from 2018 to 2024," the authors exploit Spain's December 2022 labor reform, which sharply curtailed the use of fixed-term contracts and shifted hiring toward open-ended contracts. They implement a difference-in-differences design comparing occupations with high pre-reform reliance on temporary contracts to less-exposed occupations. The reform produced a marked rise in permanent hiring in treated occupations together with a substantial increase in advertised employer-provided training: "training rose by 4.3 percentage points, fully closing the pre-reform gap by 2024." The findings provide direct, posting-level evidence for a Becker-style mechanism in which longer expected job tenure raises firms' returns to specific investments in workers, and they have clear implications for the design of dual labor markets and for the role of contract structure in human-capital formation. IZA Discussion Paper No. 18539. JEL: J24, J41, J63, J68. Keywords: temporary employment, on-the-job training, human capital investment, employment contracts.
