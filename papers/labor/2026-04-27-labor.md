# Labor Economics Papers — 2026-04-27

## 1. The Labor Market Returns to Delaying Pregnancy [Fertility]
**Authors:** Yana Gallen, Juanna Schrøter Joensen, Eva Rye Johansen, and Gregory Veramendi
**Date:** April 2026
**Source:** https://www.iza.org/publications/dp/18529/the-labor-market-returns-to-delaying-pregnancy
**Abstract:** This paper estimates the causal labor-market cost of an earlier-than-planned birth using Swedish administrative health and employment registers covering the universe of contraceptive users. The authors exploit the quasi-random timing of contraceptive failures to construct an instrument for the timing of motherhood, comparing women whose contraception failed in a given year with otherwise similar women whose contraception failed later. They find that "unplanned pregnancies halt women's career progression, resulting in income losses of 19% five years later," with effects concentrated among younger women and those still in education at the time of conception. The income penalty operates through both employment and wage margins and persists well beyond the immediate postnatal period, implying that the timing — not just the occurrence — of childbearing is a quantitatively important driver of the gender earnings gap. The results provide IV-based evidence of large dynamic returns to fertility delay and have direct implications for the design of contraceptive access, parental-leave, and childcare policies. IZA Discussion Paper No. 18529. JEL: J13, J22, J24, J31. Keywords: labor market costs of motherhood, fertility, contraceptives, unplanned pregnancy.

## 2. The Impact of Maternal Education on Early Childhood Development [Education]
**Authors:** Moriam Khanam, Mohammad Hajizadeh, and Casey Warman
**Date:** April 2026
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w35075
**Abstract:** This paper estimates the intergenerational human-capital returns to maternal schooling using Bangladesh's Female Secondary School Stipend Program — a long-running, gender-targeted conditional cash transfer that sharply expanded girls' secondary education in rural areas — as a natural experiment. The authors exploit cohort-by-region variation in stipend exposure as an instrument for completed maternal schooling and link it to standardized measures of early childhood development. They find that "one additional year of maternal schooling improves child development scores by 0.5 points on a 0–10 scale," with gains in overall developmental readiness of about 7.5 percentage points and in literacy-numeracy of about 7.7 percentage points. The effects are accompanied by improvements in child nutrition, the home learning environment, and parental engagement, suggesting that the impact of maternal education runs through both income and within-household investment channels. The results bear directly on the value of girls' education policies in low-income settings and on the long-run human-capital consequences of expanding female schooling. NBER Working Paper No. 35075. Programs: Children and Families; Development Economics; Economics of Education; Economics of Health.

## 3. Mind the Confidence Gap: Gender, Domain-Specific Self-Beliefs, and STEM Pathways [Gender]
**Authors:** Britta Hecker, Nikki Shure, and Ipek Yükselen
**Date:** April 2026
**Source:** https://www.iza.org/publications/dp/18535/mind-the-confidence-gap-gender-domain-specific-self-beliefs-and-stem-pathways
**Abstract:** Using longitudinal data on German secondary-school students linked to subsequent academic and vocational tracks, the authors investigate how subject-specific confidence shapes the gender gap in STEM participation. They estimate models that disentangle math, ICT, science, and reading self-beliefs, and show that "domain-specific confidence measures provide markedly different predictions from composite confidence indices." Mathematics and ICT confidence raise the probability of entering a STEM pathway, while reading confidence reduces it — patterns that are masked when confidence is collapsed into a single index. Because girls report systematically lower math and ICT confidence at given achievement levels, this domain mix accounts for a substantial share of the gender gap in STEM uptake; broad-based confidence-building interventions, the authors caution, may inadvertently reinforce gender disparities by raising verbal self-beliefs more than mathematical ones. The paper contributes to the literature on non-cognitive skills, occupational segregation, and the design of STEM-targeted policies. IZA Discussion Paper No. 18535. JEL: I24, I23, D91, J24, J16. Keywords: education, STEM, confidence, gender.

## 4. Trends in Health Inequalities among Spanish Retirees [Health]
**Authors:** Cristina Bellés-Obrero, Manuel Flores Mallo, Pilar García-Gómez, Sergi Jimenez-Martin, and Judit Vall Castelló
**Date:** April 2026
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w35105
**Abstract:** This paper documents how socioeconomic gradients in health among older workers and retirees evolved in Spain between 2004 and 2022, a period spanning the Great Recession, austerity-era pension reforms, and the COVID-19 shock. Using eight waves of the Survey of Health, Ageing and Retirement in Europe (SHARE) and concentration-index methods, the authors examine five outcomes: limitations in basic and instrumental activities of daily living, the number of chronic conditions, self-assessed health, and cognitive performance. They find "no clear evidence that health inequality has increased from 2004 to 2022," with income-related gradients remaining broadly stable across cohorts despite large macroeconomic and policy shocks. The results are interpreted in light of Spain's universal healthcare system and its successive pension reforms, which together appear to have insulated older workers from a widening of health inequality. The paper informs the literature on retirement, disability, and the labor-market consequences of health by showing that institutional design can stabilize the health gradient even during prolonged labor-market disruption. NBER Working Paper No. 35105. Programs: Economics of Aging; Microeconomics; Public Economics; Health, Education, and Welfare.

## 5. AI and Worker Well-Being: Evidence from a Nationally Representative Study [Health]
**Authors:** Alex Bryson, Antti Kauhanen, and Petri Rouvinen
**Date:** April 2026
**Source:** https://www.iza.org/publications/dp/18540/ai-and-worker-well-being-evidence-from-a-nationally-representative-study
**Abstract:** The paper estimates the impact of workplace artificial-intelligence adoption on worker well-being using a nationally representative Finnish panel of employees surveyed annually between 2018 and 2023, spanning the period over which generative-AI tools diffused into routine work. Identifying off within-worker variation in AI use intensity, the authors find that "AI use intensity and job satisfaction" are not consistently related — contrary to several prior cross-country studies — but that workers actively engaged with AI report higher work engagement, especially when AI is essential to their role. Reported anxieties about technology-driven displacement remained stable through the period of rapid AI advancement and did not predict subsequent employment transitions. The authors interpret the muted well-being response in light of Finland's high-trust labor relations and comprehensive social protection, arguing that institutional context shapes how the mental-health and labor-market consequences of automation materialize. The findings speak to the broader literature on technology, mental health, and productivity, and caution against extrapolating from less-protected labor markets. IZA Discussion Paper No. 18540. JEL: J28, L23. Keywords: artificial intelligence, job satisfaction, work engagement, technology-related fears, labour market transitions.
