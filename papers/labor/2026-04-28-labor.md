# Labor Economics Papers — 2026-04-28

## 1. Gender-Specific Economic Shocks and Household Bargaining Power [Gender]
**Authors:** Rania Gihleb, Osea Giuntella, and Dor Morag
**Date:** April 2026
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w35118
**Abstract:** This paper studies how labor-market shocks that alter the relative economic position of men and women translate into shifts in household decision-making, using U.S. retail-scanner data and a machine-learning index that measures how strongly individual products are associated with single-male versus single-female purchasing patterns. The authors exploit two large quasi-experimental shocks with sharply different gender incidence: industrial-robot adoption, which disproportionately depressed male wages and employment, and the fracking boom, which raised earnings for less-skilled young men. They find that robot exposure shifts household consumption "toward products predominantly purchased by single women," consistent with rising female bargaining power, while the fracking shock yields only weak evidence of movement toward male-favored goods. Aggregate spending on children's products is largely unchanged by either shock, but robot exposure reallocates child-directed spending toward goods more commonly bought for daughters than for sons, suggesting that within-household preferences over children also respond to the gender distribution of earnings. The results provide micro-level evidence on the collective model of the household and on the labor-market origins of intra-household gender inequality. NBER Working Paper No. 35118. Programs: Children and Families; Labor Studies.

## 2. Early Childcare Attendance and Cognitive Skills in Adolescence [Education]
**Authors:** Ingvild Almås, Nina Drange, Costas Meghir, and Henrik D. Zachrisson
**Date:** April 2026
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w35109
**Abstract:** This paper estimates the medium- and long-run effects of attending formal childcare in early childhood on adolescent cognitive achievement, exploiting a 2003 Norwegian policy expansion that generated quasi-random variation in slot availability for one- and two-year-olds across municipalities. Linking administrative childcare records to standardized test scores in grades 5 and 9, the authors find that "starting childcare one year earlier increases math scores by 9.7% of a standard deviation (SD) in grade 9," with persistent gains rather than fadeout. Effects are highly heterogeneous: children whose mothers lack a high school diploma gain about 28% SD in grade 9, narrowing the achievement gap with children of more-educated mothers by roughly one-third, and children of immigrant families also experience large positive effects. The findings imply that high-quality, publicly-funded early childcare can durably raise human capital and compress educational inequality, with direct implications for the design of early-childhood education and family policy. NBER Working Paper No. 35109. Programs: Children and Families; Economics of Education; Public Economics.

## 3. Effects of Expanding Contraceptive Choice: New Evidence from Virginia's Contraceptive Access Initiative [Fertility]
**Authors:** Jessica H. Kiser, Analisa Packham, Janelle Anthony, Evelyn Escobar, and Emily Yeatts
**Date:** April 2026
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w35108
**Abstract:** "In 2018, the Virginia Department of Health implemented the Contraceptive Access Initiative (CAI) to increase access to long-acting reversible contraceptives (LARCs). We use encounter-level data on contraceptive choice in participating CAI clinics and county-level natality data from 2014–2021 to estimate relative changes in LARC take-up and childbearing rates before and after the CAI. Difference-in-differences estimates indicate that the CAI reduced birth rates in participating counties by approximately 3 percent, or less than half of the effect size of other similar, state-level programs. We show that this smaller effect is likely due to existing high LARC take-up and contraceptive substitution." The paper combines clinic-level encounter data on method choice with county natality records to disentangle new LARC adoption from substitution across methods, and shows that the marginal fertility effect of expanding subsidized LARC access is bounded by pre-existing utilization. The results temper expectations from earlier state-level programs (e.g., Colorado, Delaware) and inform the design of state-funded contraceptive-access policies aimed at reducing unintended pregnancy and shaping female labor-supply trajectories. NBER Working Paper No. 35108. Programs: Children and Families; Economics of Health.

## 4. Does Employment Slow Cognitive Decline? Evidence from Labor Market Shocks [Health]
**Authors:** Noah Arman Kouchekinia, David Neumark, and Tim A. Bruckner
**Date:** April 2026
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w35117
**Abstract:** "With large gains in life expectancy, the population share of disability due to cognitive decline and dementia has substantially increased. Many older adults in the United States leave the workforce well before age 65. Correlational evidence suggests that leaving the workforce before retirement age could accelerate the pace of cognitive decline. We offer causal evidence, using HRS data for the United States, exploiting plausibly exogenous shifts in labor demand in local labor markets as a Bartik instrument for employment variation across these markets. We find substantial declines over time in cognitive scores stemming from negative labor demand shocks. These findings are concentrated among men aged 51 to 64, whose employment decisions and outcomes may be more sensitive to local labor market conditions than are these decisions or outcomes for women or for older men. Our evidence extends past work focusing narrowly on the retirement age window and provides further support to the notion that working to older ages may delay age-related cognitive decline." The authors link Health and Retirement Study respondents to local labor-demand variation constructed from national industry growth interacted with local industry mix, providing IV-style identification of the effect of employment on cognition. The paper bears directly on the literature linking work, mental health, and dementia risk, and on the labor-market consequences of policies that affect employment among older workers. NBER Working Paper No. 35117. Programs: Economics of Aging; Economics of Health; Labor Studies; Public Economics.

## 5. The Welfare Effects of Protecting Older Workers [Health]
**Authors:** Todd Morris, Stefan Staubli, and Benoit Dostie
**Date:** April 2026
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w35111
**Abstract:** This paper evaluates the labor-market and welfare consequences of bans on mandatory retirement, using the staggered roll-out of such bans across five Canadian provinces between 2005 and 2009 and linked employer–employee tax records covering the universe of Canadian workers and firms. Difference-in-differences estimates show that the bans "sharply reduce retirements at age 65," with sizable announcement effects in the years before implementation and substantial industry heterogeneity. Post-65 employment and earnings rise by at least 14%, generating gains in lifetime earnings comparable to a two-year extension of pension eligibility. Older workers respond by saving more, spouses delay retirement, and public finances improve through higher tax receipts and lower benefit outlays — with no detectable adverse mortality effects and no evidence of crowd-out of younger workers. Firms with high exposure to older workers maintain productivity and profitability, adjusting payroll costs primarily through hiring decisions rather than wage cuts. The authors conclude that protecting older workers from forced retirement was welfare-improving on net, with implications for the literature on health, disability, and labor supply at older ages and for the ongoing policy debate over pension and retirement-age reform. NBER Working Paper No. 35111. Programs: Economics of Aging; Labor Studies; Public Economics.
