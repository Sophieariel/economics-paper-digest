# Labor Economics Papers — 2026-04-29

## 1. Gender Mix and Team Performance: Evidence from Obstetrics [Gender]
**Authors:** Ambar La Forgia and Manasvini Singh
**Date:** April 2026
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w35084
**Abstract:** This paper examines how the gender composition of physician teams shapes patient outcomes, using detailed inpatient records on roughly 2.5 million U.S. births. Exploiting variation in the gender mix of obstetric care teams within hospitals, the authors find that "female-only teams achieve the best maternal outcomes, whereas male-only teams have the worst." Severe maternal complications are 15.8% higher in all-male teams and 7.1–10.8% higher in mixed-gender teams relative to all-female teams. The pattern is not driven by patient sorting, observed risk, or differences in physician seniority, and instead appears consistent with gendered norms of communication and intra-team coordination. The performance advantage of female-only teams is largest for Black patients — eliminating racial disparities in adverse outcomes within those teams — and grows under high-pressure operating conditions such as overnight shifts and high-volume days. The results contribute new evidence on the labor-market consequences of occupational gender segregation and on the productivity payoff to gender diversity in high-stakes professional teams, with direct implications for hiring, scheduling, and team-design policies in medicine and other expert-service settings. NBER Working Paper No. 35084. Programs: Economics of Health.

## 2. Nudging Parents out the Door: The Impacts of Parental Encouragement on School Choice and Test Scores [Education]
**Authors:** Guthrie Gray-Lobe, Michael Kremer, Joost de Laat, Oluchi Mbonu, and Cole Scanlon
**Date:** April 2026
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w35103
**Abstract:** This paper evaluates a low-cost SMS intervention designed to engage parents of students enrolled in low-fee Kenyan private primary schools, using a two-stage randomized controlled trial that varies treatment both at the school and at the household level. The "weekly messages offering growth-mindset support and individualized performance data" raise student test scores by an average of 0.07 standard deviations, with substantially larger effects for initially lower-performing pupils, generating "12 learning-adjusted years of schooling per US$100 spent" and ranking the program among the most cost-effective education interventions in the literature. At the same time, the intervention raises student attrition by 4.7–5.0 percentage points, with exit concentrated among higher-achieving children whose families respond to better information by moving to alternative schools. The authors develop a model in which parental information about school quality jointly determines effort within schools and sorting across schools, and show that ignoring the sorting margin can lead conventional impact evaluations to overstate or understate program value. The paper bears directly on debates over information provision, school choice, and education-policy design in low- and middle-income countries. NBER Working Paper No. 35103. Programs: Children and Families; Development Economics; Economics of Education; Industrial Organization; Labor Studies; Market Design; Organizational Economics.

## 3. Workforce Quality and Early Childhood Development at Scale [Education]
**Authors:** Sarah Cattan, Gabriella Conti, and Christine Farquharson
**Date:** April 2026
**Source:** https://www.iza.org/publications/dp/18584
**Abstract:** This paper studies why the impressive impacts of high-touch early-childhood programs in pilot studies often fade when those programs are delivered at national scale, focusing on England's Family Nurse Partnership — an intensive home-visiting program for first-time teenage mothers. Using rich administrative and child-development data on caseloads of certified family nurses, the authors document "substantial heterogeneity in workforce effectiveness" even within a tightly standardized curriculum and a credentialed workforce. A one standard deviation increase in nurse effectiveness raises children's cognitive and socio-emotional development by 0.20–0.23 standard deviations, with mediated improvements in maternal mental health and parenting behaviors. Strikingly, observable nurse qualifications and tenure are weak predictors of effectiveness; what matters is the process quality of how visits are delivered. The findings suggest that human-capital investments in front-line workers — and the management practices that support them — are central to whether early-childhood interventions retain their effects when scaled, with direct implications for the design and evaluation of early-childhood and family-policy programs. IZA Discussion Paper No. 18584.

## 4. Switching Schools: Effects of College Transfers [Education]
**Authors:** Lois Miller
**Date:** April 2026
**Source:** https://www.iza.org/publications/dp/18568
**Abstract:** This paper estimates the labor-market returns to switching colleges, using Texas administrative records linking K–12, postsecondary, and earnings data, and exploiting newly identified, unpublished GPA cutoffs that four-year colleges use to admit transfer applicants. A regression-discontinuity design around these admission thresholds delivers credibly causal estimates of the earnings consequences of two common transfer paths: from two-year community colleges to four-year institutions, and from less-resourced four-year colleges to flagship universities. The author finds that "academically marginal students who transfer from two-year to four-year colleges or from less-resourced four-year colleges to flagship colleges" do not realize the earnings gains commonly assumed in policy debates over articulation agreements and transfer pathways. The mechanisms differ across margins: community-college transfers face academic mismatch and lower completion at receiving institutions, while marginal flagship transfers shift away from high-paying STEM and business majors toward lower-return fields. The paper contributes empirical evidence on the returns to education, college quality, and the design of transfer policy in U.S. higher education. IZA Discussion Paper No. 18568. JEL: I23, I26, I24.

## 5. Exploring Trends in Severe Maternal Morbidity in the U.S. [Health]
**Authors:** Pinka Chatterji, Fangning Li, and Sara Markowitz
**Date:** April 2026
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w35062
**Abstract:** This paper investigates the sharp rise in severe maternal morbidity (SMM) in the United States and the persistent racial gap in pregnancy-related health outcomes between Black and white mothers, using nationally representative inpatient hospital discharge data from 1998–2019. The authors evaluate "a large set of variables over a long time span to let the data inform us what factors may be most influential" in driving the level and growth of SMM, including obstetric and pregnancy-related comorbidities, chronic conditions, age and parity, insurance status, hospital characteristics, and area-level socioeconomic indicators. Decomposition analysis shows that the rise in observable medical and pregnancy-related comorbidities — including hypertension, diabetes, and obesity — accounts for a substantial share of the time-series increase in SMM, while observed maternal and hospital characteristics explain little of the overall variation but a meaningful share of the Black–white gap in adverse outcomes. The findings are directly relevant to debates over health and labor-market outcomes for women of childbearing age, the design of maternal-health policy, and the use of administrative health data to track disparities. NBER Working Paper No. 35062. Programs: Children and Families; Economics of Health.
