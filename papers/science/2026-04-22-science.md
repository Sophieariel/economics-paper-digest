# Economics of Science & AI Papers — 2026-04-22

## 1. Attention (And Money) Is All You Need: Why Universities Are Struggling to Keep AI Talent [Economics of Science]
**Authors:** Ufuk Akcigit, Craig A. Chikis, Emin Dinlersoz, Nathan Goldschlag
**Date:** March 2026
**Source:** NBER Working Paper 34964 — https://www.nber.org/papers/w34964
**Abstract:** The researchers analyze a dataset connecting academic publications to U.S. Census employer-employee records tracking 42,000 AI researchers across two decades. Their findings reveal that industry increasingly attracts younger and foreign-born researchers, while gender representation improves more in academia. They document that the highest-earning industry scientists earn substantially more than academic counterparts, with this wage gap expanding significantly since 2001. The study notes that researchers transitioning to industry publish less but patent more, consistent with a shift from open science toward proprietary innovation. NBER program codes: Economics of Education, Economic Fluctuations and Growth, Labor Studies, Productivity, Innovation, and Entrepreneurship; working groups: Entrepreneurship, Innovation Policy.

## 2. Knowledge Spillovers and Local Outcomes: An Existence Proof from the Establishment of the National Labs [Economics of Science]
**Authors:** Susan Helper, Resem Makan, Daniel W. Shoag
**Date:** March 2026
**Source:** NBER Working Paper 35011 — https://www.nber.org/papers/w35011
**Abstract:** The researchers examine U.S. National Laboratories established in the 1940s–1950s to measure local knowledge spillovers from public research infrastructure. Lab locations were selected primarily for security and political reasons, rather than for existing or potential innovative capability, enabling causal analysis. Results show that lab counties experienced significant, sustained increases in patenting by non-lab inventors, with patents shifting toward laboratory research fields. Using digitized county data spanning 1936–1970, the authors document persistent gains in retail sales and household income. Census records reveal wage improvements for pre-existing residents, particularly college-educated workers. School-age cohorts exposed during lab establishment attained higher education levels, suggesting a human-capital mechanism. Notably, spillovers occurred despite extensive secrecy around early nuclear research. NBER program codes: Productivity, Innovation, and Entrepreneurship; working group: Innovation Policy.

## 3. Artificial Intelligence, Productivity, and the Workforce: Evidence from Corporate Executives [Economics of AI]
**Authors:** Salomé Baslandze, Zachary Edwards, John Graham, Ty McClure, Brent H. Meyer, Michael Sparks, Sonya R. Waddell, Daniel Weitz
**Date:** March 2026
**Source:** NBER Working Paper 34984 — https://www.nber.org/papers/w34984
**Abstract:** The researchers surveyed approximately 750 corporate leaders to examine how artificial intelligence influences workplace productivity and employment. Key findings: more than half of firms have invested in AI, though smaller companies are still adopting it. Productivity improvements are positive and sector-specific, with the strongest gains expected in 2026 in high-skill services and finance. Perceived productivity gains are larger than measured productivity gains, suggesting delayed revenue effects. Regarding employment, the study finds little evidence of near-term aggregate employment declines due to AI, though larger firms anticipate workforce reductions while smaller ones expect growth. The research identifies a compositional shift in labor demand, with routine administrative positions declining and demand for skilled technical roles increasing.

## 4. Mind the Gap: AI Adoption in Europe and the U.S. [Economics of AI]
**Authors:** Alexander Bick, Adam Blandin, David J. Deming, Nicola Fuchs-Schündeln, Jonas Jessen
**Date:** March 2026
**Source:** NBER Working Paper 34995 — https://www.nber.org/papers/w34995
**Abstract:** This research examines international survey data from 2025–2026 to reveal substantial differences in AI adoption between the U.S. and Europe, and among European nations. The study identifies that workforce demographics and firm characteristics explain part of these variations. Firm management practices and encouragement of worker AI usage correlate with adoption levels. Evidence indicates AI provides significant time savings for employees. At the aggregate level, industries with stronger AI adoption demonstrate faster productivity gains, with similar patterns observed across regions. The research does not establish causal relationships regarding employment effects. NBER program codes: Economic Fluctuations and Growth, Labor Studies, Productivity, Innovation, and Entrepreneurship; working groups: Innovation Policy, Organizational Economics, Personnel Economics.

## 5. Does Generative AI Narrow Education-Based Productivity Gaps? Evidence from a Randomized Experiment [Economics of AI]
**Authors:** Guillermo Cruces, Diego Fernández Meijide, Sebastian Galiani, Ramiro H. Gálvez, María Lombardi
**Date:** February 2026 (revised March 2026)
**Source:** NBER Working Paper 34851 — https://www.nber.org/papers/w34851
**Abstract:** The researchers conducted a randomized online experiment with 1,174 adults to determine whether generative AI reduces productivity disparities between workers with different educational backgrounds. Participants completed an incentivized, workplace-style business problem-solving task either with or without AI assistance. Key findings indicate that AI boosted productivity across all participants, with notably larger improvements for those with less education. Without AI access, higher-education participants outperformed lower-education participants by 0.548 standard deviations. With AI access, this gap narrowed to 0.139 standard deviations—representing a three-quarter reduction in the initial productivity gap. The authors conclude that generative AI lessens effective productivity differences by easing constraints that disproportionately affect lower-education workers.
