# Economics of Science & AI Papers — 2026-04-22

## 1. The Economics of Postdoctoral Researcher Positions [Economics of Science]
**Authors:** Donna K. Ginther, Joshua L. Rosenbloom
**Date:** March 2026
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w35014
**Abstract:** This research examines postdoctoral researcher growth in the U.S. scientific workforce between 1979 and 2023. The authors document a fourfold increase in postdoc numbers that outpaced growth in graduate students and faculty, attributing this expansion to fragmented science funding and the NIH budget doubling in the early 2000s. The analysis reveals that while these policies boosted both postdoc supply and demand, they ultimately deteriorated employment conditions. The study also addresses career trajectories, finding limited economic returns outside academia and declining transitions to faculty roles. The authors suggest the nation may have reached "peak postdoc" due to recent numerical declines, tighter immigration rules, and higher compensation levels, potentially reducing future research productivity.

## 2. Knowledge Spillovers and Local Outcomes: An Existence Proof from the Establishment of the National Labs [Economics of Science]
**Authors:** Susan Helper, Resem Makan, Daniel W. Shoag
**Date:** March 2026
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w35011
**Abstract:** The authors examine how establishing U.S. National Laboratories during the 1940s–1950s generated knowledge spillovers in surrounding areas. Their research design leverages the fact that lab locations were determined primarily by security and political considerations rather than existing innovation capacity, with identification of control sites through archival records of runner-up locations. Counties receiving labs experienced sustained growth in patents filed by non-lab inventors, with these patents increasingly concentrating in laboratory research domains and citing lab work. Using newly digitized county economic data spanning 1936–1970, researchers document persistent rises in retail sales and household income. Census records linking 1940–1950 data reveal wage improvements for long-term residents, particularly college-educated workers. School-age cohorts exposed to laboratory establishment achieved higher educational attainment, suggesting human capital development as a transmission mechanism. Notably, these benefits materialized despite significant secrecy surrounding early nuclear research activities.

## 3. Forecasting the Economic Effects of AI [Economics of AI]
**Authors:** Ezra Karger, Otto Kuusela, Jason Abaluck, Kevin A. Bryan, Basil Halperin, Todd R. Jones, Connacher Murphy, Philip Trammell, Matt Reynolds, Dan Mayland, Ria Viswanathan, Ananaya Mittal, Rebecca Ceppas de Castro, Josh Rosenberg, Philip Tetlock, et al.
**Date:** April 2026
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w35046
**Abstract:** The authors surveyed five distinct groups—academic economists, AI company employees, policy researchers specializing in AI, highly accurate forecasters, and the general public—regarding AI's economic impact on the U.S. The research reveals that the median respondent in each group expects substantial advances in AI capabilities by 2030, small declines in labor force participation consistent with demographic shifts, and an annual GDP growth rate of 2.5%. Under a rapid AI progress scenario, experts project GDP growth approaching 4% annually and labor force participation declining to 55% by 2050. The analysis indicates disagreement among experts stems primarily from differing assessments of highly capable AI's economic consequences rather than divergent views on AI development speed. Policy preferences vary significantly, with experts favoring targeted worker retraining while the public supports broader interventions like guaranteed employment and universal basic income.

## 4. Trade in AI-Related Products [Economics of AI]
**Authors:** Michael E. Waugh
**Date:** April 2026
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w35053
**Abstract:** This research examines patterns of worldwide commerce in AI-related goods. The author employs a large language model tool to categorize trade codes and identify products essential for building and running AI systems. Findings reveal that such products comprised 23 percent of U.S. imports in 2025, with imports expanding 73 percent since 2023. During the same timeframe, non-AI imports grew only 3 percent, with the divergence emerging in early 2024. Mexico and Taiwan together represent roughly half of U.S. trade volume in these products. Tariff policies have provided substantial relief through product-specific exemptions. The analysis indicates that without the AI expansion, the U.S. trade deficit would have been approximately $200 billion lower in 2025.

## 5. Artificial Intelligence, Productivity, and the Workforce: Evidence from Corporate Executives [Economics of AI]
**Authors:** Salomé Baslandze, Zachary Edwards, John Graham, Ty McClure, Brent H. Meyer, Michael Sparks, Sonya R. Waddell, Daniel Weitz
**Date:** March 2026
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w34984
**Abstract:** This investigation draws on survey data from approximately 750 corporate leaders to examine artificial intelligence's impact on worker productivity and employment. The researchers found substantial variation in AI implementation across organizations, with many firms already adopting the technology. Productivity improvements are positive, differ by industry sector, and are anticipated to increase throughout 2026, with particularly strong results in high-skill services and financial sectors. Rather than reflecting increased capital investment, these gains stem from increases in revenue-based total factor productivity, closely associated with innovation- and demand-oriented channels. The study identifies a noteworthy discrepancy: perceived productivity gains are larger than measured productivity gains, likely reflecting a delay in revenue realizations. Regarding employment, near-term aggregate job losses appear limited, though larger enterprises anticipate workforce reductions while smaller companies expect modest employment growth. The analysis reveals labor reallocation patterns favoring technical expertise over routine administrative roles.
