# Economics of Science & AI Papers — 2026-04-24

## 1. AI Patents in the United States and China: Measurement, Organization, and Knowledge Flows [Economics of AI]
**Authors:** Hanming Fang, Xian Gu, Hanyin Yan, Wu Zhu
**Date:** April 2026
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w35022
**Abstract:** The researchers developed a classifier to identify artificial intelligence patents, achieving 97.0% precision, 91.3% recall, and a 94.0% F1 score. Applying this tool to U.S. patents (1976–2023) and Chinese patents (2010–2023), they document rapid growth in both nations with convergence in intensity and composition over time. Organizational structures, however, differ significantly: U.S. AI patenting concentrates among large private firms in established innovation hubs, whereas China shows more geographically diffuse and institutionally diverse innovation, with stronger participation from universities and state-owned enterprises. Cross-border citation patterns indicate continued technological interdependence between the two countries rather than decoupling, suggesting that despite policy efforts, the global AI knowledge frontier remains tightly linked across the two leading producers.

## 2. Demand-Driven Technical Change: Evidence from WFH Technologies [Economics of Science]
**Authors:** Steven J. Davis, Nicholas Bloom, Mihai A. Codreanu
**Date:** April 2026
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w35083
**Abstract:** This research examines how the COVID-19 pandemic redirected innovation by analyzing 5.6 million U.S. patent applications spanning 2010-2026. The authors find that the share of patent applications advancing technologies that support work-from-home rose by about two-thirds within three years after the pandemic struck, and remained significantly elevated thereafter. The surge was concentrated in telecommunications innovations, particularly video conferencing, speech recognition, and audio processing. U.S. corporations drove the shift rather than foreign competitors or academic institutions, indicating that domestic market demand shaped corporate R&D investment priorities. The findings provide direct microdata-based evidence for the demand-pull view of technical change and quantify how a large persistent demand shock reorients the direction of innovation.

## 3. Measuring the Impact of Data Centers in the United States Economy: Monetary Damage from Air Pollution and Greenhouse Gas Emissions [Economics of AI]
**Authors:** Nicholas Z. Muller
**Date:** April 2026
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w35100
**Abstract:** The paper evaluates environmental externalities from U.S. data center electricity consumption, the principal infrastructure underpinning AI deployment. Analyzing roughly 2,800 facilities consuming approximately 250 TWh annually (5–6% of U.S. generation), the research estimates external damages at $25 billion in gross external damages (GED), with a range of $10–$33 billion. Damages concentrate geographically in Texas and Virginia, which together account for about 30% of the total. While GED represents about 5% of industry GDP, planned expansion could increase electricity demand and damages by up to 85%. The author notes that damages attributable to AI-related energy use are small relative to potential productivity gains, but that locational and grid composition choices for new data centers materially affect the social cost of AI scaling.

## 4. Real Effects of Academic Research Revisited [Economics of Science]
**Authors:** Adam B. Jaffe, Laura B. Shupp, Valentina Tartari
**Date:** March 2026
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w35017
**Abstract:** This study revisits how universities contribute to innovation and economic development through three principal channels: knowledge creation, education and training of students, and technology transfer/entrepreneurship. Drawing on new microdata that link inventions, scientists, and institutions, and applying recent quasi-experimental and machine-learning methods, the authors document causal effects of academic research operating through each of these channels. They explore variation across academic disciplines and economic sectors, demonstrating that while real effects exist on average, current evidence offers limited insight into what determines success or failure in different institutional and policy contexts. The analysis carries implications for the design of innovation policy and for evaluating the social returns to public research funding. (Note: this is a synthesis paper that integrates new empirical evidence with prior literature.)

## 5. Predicted Incrementality by Experimentation (PIE) for Ad Measurement [Economics of AI]
**Authors:** Brett R. Gordon, Robert Moakler, Florian Zettelmeyer
**Date:** April 2026
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w35044
**Abstract:** This research proposes PIE, a machine-learning method that scales causal advertising measurement beyond randomized controlled trials. The approach learns from RCT data to predict campaign-level incrementality for non-experimental campaigns using post-determined features such as test-group outcomes and conversion metrics. Using Meta's 2,226 ad experiments, PIE achieves out-of-sample R² = 0.88 for incremental conversions per dollar, substantially outperforming standard last-click attribution (R² = 0.19). In practical decision-making scenarios, PIE disagrees with RCT-based decisions only 8–12% of the time, versus 12–20% for last-click methods, suggesting that AI-driven prediction can effectively translate experimental insights to scale across larger campaign portfolios. The paper provides empirical evidence on how firms can integrate AI/ML tools with experimentation to improve causal decision-making at scale.
