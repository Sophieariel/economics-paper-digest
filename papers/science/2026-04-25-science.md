# Economics of Science & AI Papers — 2026-04-25

## 1. Forecasting the Economic Effects of AI [Economics of AI]
**Authors:** Ezra Karger, Otto Kuusela, Jason Abaluck, Kevin A. Bryan, Basil Halperin, Todd R. Jones, Connacher Murphy, Philip Trammell, Matt Reynolds, Dan Mayland, Ria Viswanathan, Ananaya Mittal, Rebecca Ceppas de Castro, Joshua Rosenberg, Philip Tetlock
**Date:** April 2026
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w35046
**Abstract:** This paper elicits forecasts of how AI will affect the U.S. economy from five distinct respondent groups: academic economists, employees at AI companies, AI policy researchers, highly accurate forecasters, and the general public. Across groups, the median respondent expects substantial advances in AI capabilities by 2030, modest declines in labor force participation consistent with demographic trends, and an annual GDP growth rate of 2.5%—above both medium-run (2.0%) and long-run (1.7%) baseline forecasts. Conditional on a "rapid" AI progress scenario, experts forecast annualized GDP growth around 4% and a labor force participation rate falling from 62% to 55% by 2050, with roughly half of that decline (about 10 million lost jobs) attributable to AI. The authors find that disagreement among experts stems primarily from differing views about AI's economic effects rather than differing predictions about technological advancement. Policy preferences also diverge: experts strongly favor targeted measures such as worker retraining, whereas the general public supports both targeted programs and broader interventions, including a job guarantee and universal basic income.

## 2. Trade in AI-Related Products [Economics of AI]
**Authors:** Michael E. Waugh
**Date:** April 2026
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w35053
**Abstract:** This paper documents facts about international trade in AI-related products. The author develops a large language model (LLM) classification tool that maps HS10 codes in U.S. trade data to products used in the construction and operation of AI infrastructure. AI-related products account for 23 percent of U.S. imports in 2025, and imports of these products have grown by 73 percent since 2023. Over the same period, imports of non-AI-related products have grown by only 3 percent, with the divergence between the two categories beginning in early 2024. Mexico is a key market on both the import and export side, and together with Taiwan these two countries account for about half of all U.S. trade in AI-related products. Trade policy has treated these products lightly, with product-level exemptions shielding much of AI-related imports from tariffs. The findings highlight how rapidly AI infrastructure has reorganized U.S. trade flows and the geographic concentration of partner countries in AI supply chains.

## 3. Tracking the Credibility Revolution across Fields [Economics of Science]
**Authors:** Paul S. Goldsmith-Pinkham
**Date:** April 2026
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w35051
**Abstract:** How far has the credibility revolution spread beyond applied microeconomics? This paper updates Currie, Kleven, and Zwiers (2020b) using approximately 44,000 papers—31,500 NBER working papers (1982–2025) and 12,300 articles from eleven top economics and finance journals (2011–2024)—measuring mentions of empirical methods through keyword matching. As of 2024, 63 percent of applied micro papers mention experimental or quasi-experimental methods, compared to 47 percent in finance and 39 percent in macro/other. Method-specific patterns differ as well: difference-in-differences and randomized control trials remain concentrated in applied micro, while regression discontinuity designs and instrumental variables have diffused more broadly. The analysis documents how methodological practices in economics evolve over time and across subfields, providing systematic evidence on the spread of credibility-revolution research designs and the heterogeneity in how different fields adopt empirical innovations.

## 4. Mind the Gap: AI Adoption in Europe and the U.S. [Economics of AI]
**Authors:** Alexander Bick, Adam Blandin, David J. Deming, Nicola Fuchs-Schündeln, Jonas Jessen
**Date:** March 2026
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w34995
**Abstract:** This paper combines international evidence from worker and firm surveys conducted in 2025 and 2026 to document large gaps in AI adoption, both between the U.S. and Europe and across European countries. In 2026, 43% of U.S. workers use AI for their job compared to 32% among European workers, and in 2025, 7% of U.S. firms use AI for production versus 4% of EU firms. Cross-country differences in worker demographics and firm composition account for an important share of these gaps. AI adoption is also closely linked to firm personnel management practices and to whether firms actively encourage AI use by workers. Micro-level evidence suggests AI generates meaningful time savings for many workers, and at the macro level, industries with higher AI adoption rates have experienced faster productivity growth in recent years, with the relationship being statistically significant and similar in magnitude in Europe and the U.S. The paper was prepared for and discussed at the Brookings Papers on Economic Activity Spring 2026 Conference.

## 5. Attention (And Money) Is All You Need: Why Universities Are Struggling to Keep AI Talent [Economics of Science]
**Authors:** Ufuk Akcigit, Craig A. Chikis, Emin Dinlersoz, Nathan Goldschlag
**Date:** March 2026
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w34964
**Abstract:** This paper studies the dramatic shift in the allocation of AI research talent from universities to large incumbent firms. The authors construct a novel dataset linking academic publication records to U.S. Census employer–employee data to track approximately 42,000 AI researchers over two decades. Industry increasingly attracts younger and foreign-born researchers, while gender representation has improved more in academia. The top 1% of publishing industry scientists now earn $1.5 million more annually than comparable academics—a fivefold increase since 2001—and rising wage premia coincide with greater sorting into large incumbent firms. Researchers who move to industry publish less but patent more, consistent with a shift from open science toward proprietary innovation. The findings document how compensation differentials, access to computational resources, and demand from a small number of frontier AI firms have reshaped scientist labor markets, with implications for university research capacity, the public-private balance of AI knowledge production, and the long-run trajectory of open science.
