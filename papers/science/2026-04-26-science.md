# Economics of Science & AI Papers — 2026-04-26

## 1. The Economics of Postdoctoral Researcher Positions [Economics of Science]
**Authors:** Donna K. Ginther and Joshua L. Rosenbloom
**Date:** April 2026 (NBER Working Paper No. 35014)
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w35014
**Abstract:** This research examines postdoctoral researchers in the U.S. scientific workforce between 1979 and 2023. The study documents a fourfold expansion in postdoc positions that exceeded growth among graduate students and faculty. The authors attribute this surge to fragmented science funding structures, particularly the NIH budget doubling in the early 2000s, which simultaneously expanded postdoc availability and demand while degrading employment conditions. The analysis reveals limited career prospects outside academia and reduced transitions to faculty positions. With declining postdoc numbers, stricter immigration policies, and increased compensation costs, the research suggests the nation may have reached "peak postdoc," potentially reducing future research productivity. The authors recommend enhanced data collection and further investigation into postdocs' contributions to scientific advancement and career trajectories.

## 2. Attention (And Money) Is All You Need: Why Universities Are Struggling to Keep AI Talent [Economics of Science]
**Authors:** Ufuk Akcigit, Craig A. Chikis, Emin Dinlersoz, and Nathan Goldschlag
**Date:** March 2026 (NBER Working Paper No. 34964)
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w34964
**Abstract:** The researchers constructed a dataset tracking 42,000 AI researchers over twenty years by linking academic publications to Census employer-employee data. They found systematic changes in the allocation of AI talent, with industry increasingly recruiting younger and foreign-born researchers. Gender representation improved more substantially in academia. A striking finding reveals that the top 1% of publishing industry scientists now earn $1.5 million more annually than comparable academics, a fivefold increase since 2001. The authors note that researchers transitioning to industry publish less but patent more, suggesting a shift from open academic science toward proprietary industrial innovation, with implications for the future trajectory and openness of AI research.

## 3. AI in Science [Economics of Science]
**Authors:** Ajay K. Agrawal, John McHale, and Alexander Oettl
**Date:** March 2026 (NBER Working Paper No. 34953)
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w34953
**Abstract:** The authors examine how artificial intelligence affects scientific knowledge production. They characterize AI as an "augmentation" tool that enables "enhanced search over combinatorial spaces" rather than full automation, resulting in increased scientific productivity. The paper decomposes knowledge production into multiple stages to explain the "jagged frontier" of AI in science, showing that different tools yield varying returns across domains and workflow stages. For example, design aids like AlphaFold show strong returns, while question-generation tools show subtler impacts. The authors argue human judgment remains essential for tasks requiring abductive inference, contextual understanding, and trade-off analysis—particularly in data-sparse environments. Using a task-based model distinguishing ordinary from AI-expert scientists, they demonstrate how AI improvements generate nonlinear productivity gains amplified by the proportion of AI-expert scientists, emphasizing the importance of complementary factors like skills training and organizational design. (Note: this paper combines a conceptual task-based framework with empirical illustrations from across scientific fields.)

## 4. Artificial Intelligence, Productivity, and the Workforce: Evidence from Corporate Executives [Economics of AI]
**Authors:** Salomé Baslandze, Zachary Edwards, John Graham, Ty McClure, Brent H. Meyer, Michael Sparks, Sonya R. Waddell, and Daniel Weitz
**Date:** March 2026 (NBER Working Paper No. 34984)
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w34984
**Abstract:** This research examines AI's effects on productivity and employment using survey data from approximately 750 corporate leaders. Key findings indicate significant variation in AI adoption across companies, with over half having already made investments. The study reveals positive productivity gains that differ by sector, with the strongest effects in high-skill services and finance. Rather than resulting from increased capital spending, these gains stem from improvements in total factor productivity linked to innovation and demand-related factors. The authors identify a productivity paradox where executives perceive larger gains than measured results show, attributing this to delays in revenue recognition. Regarding employment, the research finds limited near-term aggregate job losses, though larger enterprises anticipate workforce reductions while smaller companies expect modest employment growth. Labor market shifts show declining routine clerical positions alongside increased demand for skilled technical roles.

## 5. Measuring the Impact of Data Centers in the United States Economy: Monetary Damage from Air Pollution and Greenhouse Gas Emissions [Economics of AI]
**Authors:** Nicholas Z. Muller
**Date:** April 2026 (NBER Working Paper No. 35100)
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w35100
**Abstract:** The research quantifies environmental externalities from data center electricity consumption, analyzing approximately 2,800 facilities. Key findings indicate data centers consume roughly 250 TWh annually—representing 5–6% of U.S. electricity generation—and generate approximately $25 billion in gross external damages (GED), with a range of $10–$33 billion. Damages concentrate geographically, with Texas and Virginia accounting for 30% of totals. The study notes that damages attributable to AI-related energy use are small relative to potential productivity gains, and projects that expansion could increase electricity demand by up to 85% near-term. The analysis provides the first comprehensive estimate of the social costs of the rapidly growing AI compute infrastructure in the United States.
