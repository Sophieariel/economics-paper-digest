# Economics of Science & AI Papers — 2026-04-27

## 1. Knowledge Spillovers and Local Outcomes: An Existence Proof from the Establishment of the National Labs [Economics of Science]
**Authors:** Susan Helper, Resem Makan, and Daniel W. Shoag
**Date:** March 2026 (NBER Working Paper No. 35011)
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w35011
**Abstract:** This research examines how the creation of U.S. National Laboratories during the 1940s–1950s generated local economic benefits through knowledge spillovers. The study leverages a natural experiment in which lab locations were determined by security and political reasons, rather than existing or potential innovative capability, enabling causal identification of spillover effects. The findings reveal multiple types of spillovers in laboratory counties compared to control areas. On patent activity, non-lab inventors significantly increased their patenting, with patents shifting toward laboratory research fields and increasingly citing lab patents. On economic growth, sustained increases in retail sales and household income are documented through newly digitized county data spanning 1936–1970. On wages, Census records show earnings improvements for pre-existing residents, particularly college-educated workers. On human capital, school-age cohorts exposed to lab establishment attained higher education levels. Notably, these benefits persisted despite extensive secrecy around early nuclear research, suggesting that physical proximity to public research infrastructure generates meaningful local economic gains even under restricted information conditions.

## 2. From Free Rider to Innovator: The Rise of China's Drug Development [Economics of Science]
**Authors:** Panle Jia Barwick, Hongyuan Xia, and Tianli Xia
**Date:** March 2026 (NBER Working Paper No. 34977)
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w34977
**Abstract:** This research examines how China transformed from a pharmaceutical "free rider" into a global innovation leader over the past decade. The authors compiled comprehensive data spanning the drug development supply chain—including scientific publications, clinical trials, and drug milestones across China, the U.S., and Europe. Their key finding identifies the National Reimbursement Drug List (NRDL) reform as the primary driver of this transformation. Following the reform, China saw an 86% increase in clinical trial quantity, with growth concentrated in innovative drug categories and domestic firms. The study reveals that the NRDL reform explains 43% of growth in oncology trial activity—significantly more than upstream knowledge gains can account for. Notably, dynamic gains from induced innovation exceed the reform's static gains by threefold, highlighting how market incentives drive long-term pharmaceutical innovation beyond immediate affordability improvements. The paper provides empirical evidence on how procurement and reimbursement policy can reshape national R&D trajectories.

## 3. How (un)Stable Are LLM Occupational Exposure Scores? Evidence from Multi-Model Replication [Economics of AI]
**Authors:** Michelle Yin, Hoa Vu, and Claudia Persico
**Date:** April 2026 (NBER Working Paper No. 35110)
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w35110
**Abstract:** This research examines the reliability of using large language models to measure occupational exposure to AI in labor market studies. The authors replicated a widely-used assessment method across three frontier LLM models and found significant inconsistencies. They document a 3.6-fold divergence in mean exposure with cross-model agreement as low as 57%. When applied to downstream economic analyses, individual-level coefficient magnitudes vary 2.4-fold across annotators, and county-level estimates produce conflicting results depending on which model conducted the assessment. The authors formalize this as non-classical measurement error, cautioning against treating evolving LLMs as static instruments. The study highlights critical measurement fragility in a rapidly growing body of empirical research that attempts to quantify AI's labor market impacts through LLM-based occupational exposure scores, and offers methodological guidance for researchers using LLMs as measurement tools in economics.

## 4. Mind the Gap: AI Adoption in Europe and the U.S. [Economics of AI]
**Authors:** Alexander Bick, Adam Blandin, David J. Deming, Nicola Fuchs-Schündeln, and Jonas Jessen
**Date:** March 2026 (NBER Working Paper No. 34995)
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w34995
**Abstract:** This research documents large gaps in AI adoption, both between the United States and Europe and across European countries, using harmonized worker- and firm-level survey data from 2025–2026. The study identifies that demographic and firm composition differences explain a meaningful portion of these disparities, but a substantial residual gap remains. Firm personnel management practices and active managerial encouragement of AI use correlate strongly with adoption rates at the worker level. Workers report substantial time savings from AI implementation. Industries with higher AI adoption have experienced faster productivity growth, with effects of similar magnitude in both regions. The authors find no clear association between industry-level AI adoption and contemporaneous employment changes. The paper acknowledges data limitations and recommends priorities for future research to better understand AI's productivity and labor market impacts in cross-country perspective.

## 5. The Economics of Algorithmic Personalization: Evidence from an Educational Technology Platform [Economics of AI]
**Authors:** Keshav Agrawal, Susan Athey, Ayush Kanodia, Shanjukta Nath, and Emil Palikot
**Date:** March 2026 (NBER Working Paper No. 34950)
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w34950
**Abstract:** This study investigates whether personalized recommendations powered by machine learning can boost engagement in educational technology. The researchers implemented a collaborative filtering recommender system on Freadom, an English-learning app serving Indian children. A randomized controlled trial with 7,750 students found that personalization increased engagement by 60% in the targeted content section and 14% across the entire app. Using a regression discontinuity design to track five-month deployment effects, the scaled impact exceeded initial trial estimates by 2.5 times—the opposite of the typical implementation decay observed when experimental interventions are scaled up. However, the benefits proved short-lived. Gains concentrated in users' initial weeks, with diminishing returns following. The authors conclude that content availability rather than algorithmic sophistication becomes the binding constraint, suggesting that expanded content libraries matter more than further algorithmic improvements for sustained engagement on AI-driven recommendation platforms.
