# Economics of Science & AI Papers — 2026-04-28

## 1. Attention (And Money) Is All You Need: Why Universities Are Struggling to Keep AI Talent [Economics of Science]
**Authors:** Ufuk Akcigit, Craig A. Chikis, Emin Dinlersoz, and Nathan Goldschlag
**Date:** March 2026 (NBER Working Paper No. 34964)
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w34964
**Abstract:** This research tracks the careers of approximately 42,000 AI specialists over two decades using linked publication and employment records to understand the migration of scientific talent between academia and industry. The authors document a marked acceleration of industry hiring, with firms increasingly recruiting younger and internationally-trained AI scientists, while academia has shown improvements in gender diversity but losses at the upper tail of productivity. Compensation differentials have widened sharply: the top 1% of publishing industry scientists now earn $1.5 million more annually than comparable academics, a fivefold increase since 2001. Scientists transitioning from academia to industry produce fewer peer-reviewed publications but generate substantially more patents, suggesting a rotation of frontier AI knowledge from publicly-shared research toward proprietary corporate development. The paper provides empirical evidence that financial premia and concentrated industrial demand for AI talent are reshaping the scientist labor market, with implications for the future direction and openness of basic AI research conducted within universities.

## 2. AI Patents in the United States and China: Measurement, Organization, and Knowledge Flows [Economics of Science]
**Authors:** Hanming Fang, Xian Gu, Hanyin Yan, and Wu Zhu
**Date:** April 2026 (NBER Working Paper No. 35022)
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w35022
**Abstract:** The authors construct a fine-tuned classifier for identifying artificial intelligence patents by refining the PatentSBERTa model with manually labeled USPTO data, achieving 97.0% precision, 91.3% recall, and a 94.0% F1 score, and apply it to U.S. patents from 1976 to 2023 and Chinese patents from 2010 to 2023. The data document rapid AI patent growth in both countries with converging intensity and subject-area composition, while China has recently surpassed the United States in annual patent counts. The organization of innovation differs sharply: U.S. AI patenting concentrates among large corporate players in established innovation regions, whereas Chinese AI patenting is more geographically dispersed and features substantially greater participation by universities and state-owned enterprises. For publicly listed firms, AI patents are associated with measurable financial value premia in both countries. Citation patterns reveal continued cross-border knowledge linkages rather than decoupling, with Chinese inventors drawing especially heavily on cutting-edge U.S. research. The paper provides new empirical evidence on the production, organization, and international diffusion of AI knowledge.

## 3. Forecasting the Economic Effects of AI [Economics of AI]
**Authors:** Ezra Karger, Otto Kuusela, Jason Abaluck, Kevin A. Bryan, Basil Halperin, Todd R. Jones, Connacher Murphy, Philip Trammell, Matt Reynolds, Dan Mayland, Ria Viswanathan, Ananaya Mittal, Rebecca Ceppas de Castro, Josh Rosenberg, and Philip Tetlock
**Date:** April 2026 (NBER Working Paper No. 35046)
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w35046
**Abstract:** This study elicits and compares forecasts on the economic impact of AI on the U.S. economy through 2050 from five distinct groups: academic economists, AI company employees, policy researchers, highly accurate "superforecasters," and the general public. Across groups, respondents anticipate substantial advances in AI capabilities by 2030 and project annual GDP growth around 2.5%, exceeding standard baseline forecasts. Under a rapid AI progress scenario, expert respondents forecast GDP growth around 4% and a decline in labor force participation to 55% by 2050, with roughly 10 million additional jobs displaced as a result of AI. The largest disagreements among forecasters concern the economic effects of highly capable AI systems rather than the pace of AI development itself. Policy preferences also diverge: experts favor worker retraining programs while the public is more supportive of broader interventions such as guaranteed employment and universal basic income. The paper provides systematic empirical evidence on heterogeneity in expectations about AI's macroeconomic and labor market consequences.

## 4. Trade in AI-Related Products [Economics of AI]
**Authors:** Michael E. Waugh
**Date:** April 2026 (NBER Working Paper No. 35053)
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w35053
**Abstract:** This paper documents the rapid expansion of international trade in AI-related products, using a large language model to classify Harmonized System trade codes according to their relationship to artificial intelligence inputs and outputs. The analysis shows that AI-related products accounted for 23 percent of U.S. imports in 2025, with imports of these goods growing 73 percent since 2023 compared with only 3 percent growth for non-AI products over the same period. Mexico and Taiwan together account for approximately half of U.S. AI-related trade. Recent trade policy has provided product-level tariff exemptions for many AI items, suggesting that policymakers have implicitly recognized the strategic importance of AI inputs. Counterfactual estimates indicate that the U.S. goods trade deficit would have been nearly $200 billion smaller in 2025 in the absence of the AI-related import boom. The paper provides new empirical evidence on the scale, geography, and policy treatment of AI-related global trade.

## 5. Measuring the Impact of Data Centers in the United States Economy: Monetary Damage from Air Pollution and Greenhouse Gas Emissions [Economics of AI]
**Authors:** Nicholas Z. Muller
**Date:** April 2026 (NBER Working Paper No. 35100)
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w35100
**Abstract:** This research quantifies the environmental externalities generated by U.S. data center electricity consumption, a load that has expanded rapidly with the diffusion of generative AI and cloud services. Using detailed facility-level data on roughly 2,800 operational data centers consuming approximately 250 TWh annually—about 5 to 6 percent of U.S. electricity generation—the analysis combines emissions inventories with integrated assessment models to estimate gross external damages from air pollution and greenhouse gas emissions of $25 billion, with a plausible range of $10–33 billion. Damages are highly geographically concentrated, with Texas and Virginia together accounting for roughly 30 percent of national totals. Planned data center expansion could increase electricity demand and associated environmental damages by up to 85 percent in the near term. The paper provides empirical evidence that AI-related infrastructure imposes substantial but spatially uneven environmental costs, while noting that these damages remain modest relative to the potential productivity benefits of AI deployment.
