# Economics of Science & AI Papers — 2026-04-29

## 1. China's Global Ownership [Economics of Science]
**Authors:** Jennie Bai, Luc Laeven, Yaojun Ke, and Hong Ru
**Date:** April 2026 (NBER Working Paper No. 35106)
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w35106
**Abstract:** This research examines Chinese corporate ownership across global markets by reconstructing ownership chains through offshore entities for 161,773 firms in 159 countries over 2012–2021, revealing holdings substantially larger than official foreign direct investment statistics suggest. Chinese-controlled foreign assets grew at roughly 20% annually, reaching approximately $2.1 trillion by 2021—about 3% of global corporate assets. State-owned enterprises concentrate acquisitions in R&D-intensive and supply-chain-linked firms, and after acquisition target companies raise capital and R&D spending but experience stagnant patent output and declining profitability. Chinese parent companies, by contrast, exhibit accelerated patent growth following acquisitions in developed economies, a pattern the authors describe as "innovation spillback." Increased Chinese presence is associated with reduced R&D investment among competitor firms, though aggregate industry innovation levels remain stable. The paper characterizes China's global ownership strategy as distinctly state-driven, prioritizing long-term technological absorption and capacity building over near-term financial performance, and provides new empirical evidence on how cross-border ownership shapes the international diffusion of R&D and innovation.

## 2. Postdoctoral Mobility and Returnees' Careers in Italian Academia [Economics of Science]
**Authors:** Massimiliano Coda Zabetta and Aldo Geuna
**Date:** 2026 (Research Policy, Volume 55, Issue 4, Article 105456)
**Source:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.respol.2026.105456
**Abstract:** This study examines the relationship between international postdoctoral stays and academic career advancement among researchers returning to the Italian university system. Using a 30-year panel of Italian PhD holders linked to publication and academic-position records, the authors analyze how foreign postdoctoral experience affects two career milestones: time to Assistant Professor appointment and subsequent promotion to Associate Professor. Postdoctoral returnees take longer to obtain an Assistant Professorship, but, conditional on productivity, are subsequently promoted faster. Postdoctoral stays longer than 18 months at prestigious foreign host institutions are particularly associated with faster promotion in non-inbreeding universities, and the effect is most pronounced for researchers who change institutions at the time of promotion. Maintaining domestic publishing networks facilitates initial academic entry, while sustained international collaborations from the postdoctoral period enhance promotion prospects. The Italian academic labor market has increasingly rewarded research quality and international mobility among recent PhD cohorts, providing new empirical evidence on the returns to international scientific mobility in a continental European academic system.

## 3. Specialisation and the Career Outcomes of Inventors [Economics of Science]
**Authors:** Jordan Bisset
**Date:** 2026 (Research Policy, Volume 55, Issue 5, Article 105477)
**Source:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.respol.2026.105477
**Abstract:** Recent years have seen a reorganization of innovation production toward groups of collaborators and increasingly specialized inventors. This paper constructs a panel of highly prolific inventors observed frequently throughout their careers and develops a framework in which inventors face a trade-off between acquiring knowledge depth and knowledge breadth. Empirically, after controlling for selection into specialized careers, knowledge depth is consistently negatively associated with the probability that an inventor contributes to an invention which receives citations in the top quantile. The negative relationship is robust across technological fields and inventor career stages, and is consistent with knowledge obsolescence eroding the productivity of highly specialized inventors over time. The author argues that because top-cited inventions deliver disproportionate social value, the disadvantage faced by specialized inventors has potentially important implications for aggregate innovation outcomes and for the design of human-capital and team-composition policies in research-intensive organizations.

## 4. AI Users Are Not All Alike: The Characteristics of French Firms Buying and Developing AI [Economics of AI]
**Authors:** Flavio Calvino and Luca Fontanelli
**Date:** 2026 (Research Policy, Volume 55, Issue 5, Article 105473)
**Source:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.respol.2026.105473
**Abstract:** This paper investigates French firms using artificial intelligence in 2018 and explores the link between AI use and productivity, distinguishing AI users that source AI from external providers ("AI buyers") from those developing their own AI systems ("AI developers"), based on the official French ICT survey. AI buyers tend to be larger than non-users, but this difference vanishes when accounting for ICT and digital characteristics. AI developers are larger and younger than non-users even controlling for ICT factors, and they leverage more specialized ICT skills, including data scientists. Digital technologies, digital skills, and digital infrastructure all play a key role for AI use. Overall, AI users are more productive than non-users, but this gap appears to be largely driven by selection. Once endogeneity is addressed through multiple analytical approaches, AI use positively affects developers' productivity beyond such selection dynamics, while productivity effects for AI buyers are more muted. The paper provides new firm-level empirical evidence that the heterogeneity between AI buyers and AI developers matters for understanding the economic effects of AI adoption.

## 5. Digital Skill Adoption and Employment Dynamics: Evidence from Matched Vacancy–Employer–Employee Data [Economics of AI]
**Authors:** Mathias Fjællegaard Jensen and Cédric Schneider
**Date:** 2026 (Research Policy, Volume 55, Issue 5, Article 105476)
**Source:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.respol.2026.105476
**Abstract:** This paper examines how firms' investments in digital skills affect employment dynamics in the Danish labor market. Using natural language processing on the universe of online job postings linked to matched employer–employee administrative data, the authors construct firm-level measures of demand for distinct digital capabilities and document a strong positive association between digital skill demand—particularly in data science and artificial intelligence—and firm productivity. Dynamic specifications show that increases in digital-competency demand are followed by expansions in firm-level employment, with gains spread across multiple demographic groups but concentrated in occupations requiring data-science and AI expertise. While digitalization is generally job-creating and productivity-enhancing at the firm level, the effects vary substantially by technology type, and the adoption of data-science and AI skills appears to widen existing disparities across occupations, industries, and geographic regions. The paper provides new empirical evidence that the labor-market consequences of AI and digital adoption operate primarily through firm-level skill demand, with implications for inequality in the diffusion of AI-related work.
