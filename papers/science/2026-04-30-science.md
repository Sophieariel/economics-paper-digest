# Economics of Science & AI Papers — 2026-04-30

## 1. Forecasting the Economic Effects of AI [Economics of AI]
**Authors:** Ezra Karger, Otto Kuusela, Jason Abaluck, Kevin A. Bryan, Basil Halperin, Todd R. Jones, Connacher Murphy, Philip Trammell, Matt Reynolds, Dan Mayland, Ria Viswanathan, Ananaya Mittal, Rebecca Ceppas de Castro, Josh Rosenberg, and Philip E. Tetlock
**Date:** April 2026 (NBER Working Paper No. 35046)
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w35046
**Abstract:** This paper compares structured forecasts of the economic effects of artificial intelligence elicited from five distinct populations: academic economists, AI-company employees, AI policy researchers, "superforecasters" with established track records of accuracy, and the general public. The authors collect probabilistic predictions across multiple AI-capability and macroeconomic outcomes through 2050. Respondents anticipate substantial advances in AI capabilities by 2030 and a baseline annual U.S. GDP growth rate of about 2.5%; under a rapid-AI-progress scenario, the median forecaster expects roughly 4% annualized GDP growth and a decline in labor-force participation to 55% by 2050—corresponding to approximately 10 million job losses attributable to AI. Decomposing disagreement, the authors show that variation across forecaster groups is driven primarily by differing views on the economic consequences of highly capable AI rather than by differing predictions about the pace of AI progress itself. Policy preferences also diverge sharply: experts favor targeted worker retraining, while the general public supports both targeted programs and broader interventions such as job guarantees and universal basic income. The paper provides new empirical evidence on the structure of expert and lay expectations about AI's macroeconomic and labor-market effects.

## 2. How (un)Stable Are LLM Occupational Exposure Scores? Evidence from Multi-Model Replication [Economics of AI]
**Authors:** Michelle Yin, Hoa Vu, and Claudia Persico
**Date:** April 2026 (NBER Working Paper No. 35110)
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w35110
**Abstract:** A growing literature uses large language models (LLMs) to generate occupational AI-exposure scores that are then used as inputs in econometric analyses of labor-market effects. This paper investigates the reliability of such scores by replicating an established occupational-exposure measurement task across three frontier LLMs holding prompts and tasks identical. The authors document substantial measurement instability, including a 3.6-fold divergence in mean exposure across models and pairwise agreement rates as low as 57%. The instability propagates to downstream regression results: estimated coefficients on AI exposure vary by a factor of 2.4 across annotator models, and regional-level estimates flip from statistically significant negative to insignificant positive depending on which LLM performs the assessment. The findings raise concerns about treating evolving LLMs as consistent measurement instruments in empirical economics and suggest that researchers using LLM-generated exposure scores should report multi-model replications, document model and prompt versions, and explicitly account for measurement uncertainty when drawing inferences about AI's labor-market effects.

## 3. Artificial Intelligence, Productivity, and the Workforce: Evidence from Corporate Executives [Economics of AI]
**Authors:** Salomé Baslandze, Zachary Edwards, John R. Graham, Ty McClure, Brent H. Meyer, Michael Sparks, Sonya R. Waddell, and Daniel Weitz
**Date:** March 2026 (NBER Working Paper No. 34984; revised April 27, 2026)
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w34984
**Abstract:** The authors use novel survey data from approximately 750 corporate executives in the U.S. to study the effects of artificial intelligence on firm-level productivity and the workforce. AI adoption is widespread but uneven: more than half of firms have already invested in AI, while smaller firms are still beginning implementation. The paper documents positive labor-productivity gains from AI that vary by sector—largest in high-skill services and finance—and that are expected to strengthen further in 2026. These productivity gains are not driven primarily by capital deepening but by increases in revenue-based total factor productivity, suggesting that innovation- and demand-oriented channels matter as much as capital investment. There is little evidence of near-term aggregate employment declines, although larger firms anticipate workforce reductions while smaller firms expect modest growth. Job composition is shifting both within and across firms, with routine clerical roles declining and relative demand for skilled technical roles rising. The paper also documents a disconnect between executives' perceived productivity improvements and contemporaneously measured gains, which the authors attribute to delayed revenue realization. The study provides new empirical evidence on AI's near-term economic effects from the vantage point of decision-makers inside firms.

## 4. Attention (And Money) Is All You Need: Why Universities Are Struggling to Keep AI Talent [Economics of Science]
**Authors:** Ufuk Akcigit, Craig A. Chikis, Emin Dinlersoz, and Nathan Goldschlag
**Date:** March 2026 (NBER Working Paper No. 34964)
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w34964
**Abstract:** This paper constructs a novel dataset that links academic publication records to U.S. Census employer–employee data to track approximately 42,000 AI researchers over two decades and document the reallocation of AI talent between academia and industry. The share of AI researchers working in industry rose from 48% in 2001 to 68% by 2019, with industry increasingly attracting younger and foreign-born researchers, while gender representation has improved more in academia than in industry. The top 1% of publishing industry scientists now earn approximately $1.5 million more annually than comparable academics, a roughly fivefold increase since 2001. The U.S.-born share of AI researchers in industry has fallen by 5.5 percentage points, with that decline almost entirely accounted for by rising shares of researchers from China (+3.8 percentage points) and India (+2.0 percentage points). Researchers who move from academia to industry publish less but patent more, consistent with a shift from open science toward proprietary innovation. The findings provide new evidence on how compensation, immigration, and concentrated firm-level demand for AI scientists are reshaping the U.S. scientific workforce, with potentially important consequences for the long-run trajectory of academic AI research.

## 5. Knowledge Spillovers and Local Outcomes: An Existence Proof from the Establishment of the National Labs [Economics of Science]
**Authors:** Susan Helper, Resem Makan, and Daniel W. Shoag
**Date:** March 2026 (NBER Working Paper No. 35011)
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w35011
**Abstract:** This paper provides causal evidence on knowledge spillovers from public R&D by exploiting the establishment of U.S. National Laboratories during the 1940s and 1950s. Using archival records, the authors identify counties that were "runner-up" candidates considered but ultimately not selected for lab placement, and use these as a credible counterfactual for treated lab counties. Non-laboratory inventors located in lab counties experience sustained increases in patenting activity following lab establishment, and the technological composition of their patents shifts toward areas of laboratory research, indicating local diffusion of scientific knowledge despite the substantial secrecy surrounding early nuclear research. County-level data spanning 1936–1970 show persistent growth in retail sales and household income; census records reveal wage gains for college-educated workers; and school-age cohorts exposed to lab establishment attain higher educational levels in adulthood. The paper interprets these results as an "existence proof" that co-location with public R&D facilities can generate sizable, durable local economic benefits even when formal information flows are heavily restricted. The findings are relevant for ongoing debates about the geographic returns to public science investment and place-based innovation policy.
